## The Master Algorithm for Solving Business Problems

This is the most important algorithm of the entire course. It encompasses all mechanics, all business tasks, and the complete methodology.

### Step 1: Define the Business Task
The business task is ALWAYS primary. Before touching the job graph, before research, before anything -- clarify what business outcome you need.

**Four categories of business tasks** (with sub-types):

| Category | Business Tasks |
|----------|---------------|
| Market Entry & Positioning | Exit direct competition; Enter product to market; Increase market size |
| Value Creation | Create product value (first priority); Grow product value |
| Acquisition & Channels | Get acquisition channel; Scale acquisition |
| Monetization & Retention | Grow conversion; Grow average check; Grow return rate; Reduce churn |

Each task has a specific set of applicable mechanics (total: 34 mechanics across all tasks).

### Step 2: Find Applicable Mechanics
For the defined business task, look up which mechanics can help. Each business task has a filtered subset of the 34 total mechanics.

**What mechanics do**: They tell you WHERE in the job graph the answer to your business task might be found. Mechanics are a navigation tool -- they direct your attention to the right part of the graph.

Example mechanics for "Grow Average Check":
- Communicate that you execute higher-level jobs (product does X, Y, Z beyond the basic job)
- Satisfy deep psychological needs (e.g., Tinkoff Premium selling status)
- Shift to a higher-spending segment
- Shift to premium positioning
- Bundle with additional value
- ...and more (up to 11 mechanics for this task)

### Step 3: Gather Raw Data (Research)
Once you know which mechanics to test, you know WHERE in the graph to look. Now gather information:
- Conduct AJTBD interviews (use appropriate guide modules)
- Buy market reports
- Talk to domain experts
- Study competing solutions
- Quantitatively validate what you find qualitatively

**Critical**: You know what to look for before you start researching. You're not randomly exploring -- you're directed by the mechanics.

Research targets by mechanic direction:
- If near the Core Job: search for the Core Job itself
- If deep needs: study the psychological need codes (e.g., what does "status" mean for this segment?)
- If other segments: search for segments with higher income, different jobs, or B2B if you're B2C (and vice versa)
- If activating knowledge: study what information triggers job awakening

### Step 4: Apply Mechanics, Generate Hypotheses
From the raw data, apply each mechanic. Not all will work -- from 11 mechanics, maybe 2-4 will produce viable hypotheses.

Output: A list of hypotheses. Examples:
- "We can enter segment X"
- "We can communicate that we execute higher-level jobs"
- "We can add status element"
- "We can expand into B2B"
- "We can awaken sleeping jobs by delivering activating knowledge Y"

### Step 5: Rank Hypotheses Using RICE
Rank all surviving hypotheses using the RICE framework:
- **R** (Reach): How many people will this hypothesis affect?
- **I** (Impact): How much will this move the target metric?
- **C** (Confidence): How confident are we in this hypothesis?
- **E** (Effort): How much will it cost us?

**Critical nuance**: The Impact scale is DIFFERENT for each business task. For "Grow Average Check", impact = how much can this raise the average check (scale from current to maximum realistic). For "Grow Conversion", impact = how much can this improve conversion rate.

Formula: RICE Score = (Reach x Impact x Confidence) / Effort

### Summary Flow
```
Business Task -> Applicable Mechanics -> Research (directed by mechanics) ->
Apply Mechanics to Data -> Generate Hypotheses -> Rank by RICE -> Execute top hypotheses
```

## The Two Long-term Mechanics

On a decades-long horizon, only TWO mechanics determine product evolution:

### 1. Kill Jobs
Eliminate lower-level jobs so people accomplish the higher-level job with less effort. Every major product innovation in history has killed jobs.

### 2. Move to Higher-level Jobs
As you kill enough lower-level jobs, your product naturally moves to compete for higher-level jobs. The higher you go, the more value you create.

**These two are recursive**: Killing jobs -> moving higher -> killing jobs at the new level -> moving higher again. This is structurally similar to TRIZ's ideal end state: "The task is accomplished; the system doesn't exist."

All other 32 mechanics are, in some form, variants or sub-cases of these two.

## Segmentation by Jobs

### Core Principle: Segment by Past Behavior, Never by Future Intent
People are terrible at predicting their own future behavior. With high probability, stated future intentions are false jobs.

**The only valid segmentation signal**: The person SPENT time, money, or energy in the PAST on this job. This is confirmed in 95-98%+ of cases.

**How to find segments**:
1. Find people who executed a similar job in the past with some solution
2. Study these jobs, identify patterns
3. Group by jobs/job combinations into segments
4. Study satisfaction with various solutions within each segment
5. Base your strategy on the assumption that similar people will continue to appear (daily, weekly, monthly, yearly)

### Segmentation by Core Jobs
Group people by the highest-level job they're trying to accomplish. This reveals the broadest competitive landscape and the deepest motivational drivers.

Example: People buying mattresses. Core Job is NOT "buy a mattress." It might be "continue functioning when sleep-deprived during a stressful work period." This redefines the entire competitive set.

**When to use**: Finding new markets, redefining competitive positioning, creating breakthrough value propositions.

### Segmentation by Big Jobs
Group people by the high-level job your product competes for. More specific than Core Jobs, more actionable for product decisions.

Example: For a training course, Big Job might be "launch a new product" or "restart growth in a competitive market." Different Big Jobs = different segments with different needs.

**When to use**: Choosing target segments for existing products, designing value propositions, crafting marketing messages.

### Segmentation by Job Combinations (Graph Segments)
Group people by a specific COMBINATION of jobs from the graph. Most powerful for detailed product design.

Example: Business club members might be segmented by job combination: "hire employees through networking" + "socialize with peers who understand entrepreneurship" + "get inspired by successful entrepreneurs" = one segment, because they share a specific graph pattern.

**When to use**: Detailed product design, feature prioritization, onboarding flow design.

### ABCD Segmentation (By Margin + Satisfaction)
Overlay segmentation:
- **A**: High-margin, high-satisfaction. Focus here.
- **B**: Medium-margin, good satisfaction. Secondary focus.
- **C**: Low-margin, unsatisfied. Likely bought by mistake. Deprioritize.
- **D**: Generating losses. Actively filter out.

**When to use**: Existing products optimizing for profitability and customer success.

### Segment Refresh Rate
Segments change over time. External events (pandemic, war, AI disruption) accelerate change.

Recommended refresh frequency:
- Mass B2C products: every 1-1.5 years
- B2B products: potentially faster now due to AI disruption
- Stable markets: every 1.5-2 years

Within the refresh period, segmentation knowledge is reliable enough to build strategy on.

## Critical Sequences in Job Graphs

### What is a Critical Sequence
The ordered chain of sequential jobs a person MUST complete to reach the value point. If the person drops off at ANY step, they never get value and leave for a competing solution.

### How to Identify Critical Sequences
1. Map the sequential jobs from "first contact" to "received value"
2. For each step, measure/estimate drop-off rate
3. The EARLIEST high-drop-off step is the bottleneck

### The Drop-off Principle
When a person drops off the critical sequence:
- The Big Job REMAINS (the person still wants the outcome)
- They switch to a COMPETING SOLUTION for the Big Job
- The drop-off is caused by: insufficient motivation (wrong segment) OR barriers/problems (fixable)

### Fixing Critical Sequences
**Priority**: Fix the EARLIEST blocking step. Later steps don't matter if people never reach them.

**Diagnostic question**: Is the drop-off from low motivation or from barriers?
- Low motivation: You're targeting the wrong segment. Find people who DID complete this step in the past.
- Barriers: Fix the barrier. Deliver activating knowledge. Simplify the step. Kill intermediate jobs.

**Example**: Psychotherapy matching service. Critical sequence: realize I need therapy -> decide to search -> find a therapist -> book session -> attend session -> evaluate -> continue. Most people drop off at step 1-2 (fear, uncertainty). Fixing step 1-2 (delivering activating knowledge about therapy benefits) moves more people to step 4-5 where the product actually operates.

### Critical Sequence and Activating Knowledge
Activating knowledge can "shortcut" the critical sequence by:
1. Helping people skip orientation jobs (they already know the answer)
2. Reducing barriers at early steps
3. Eliminating the need to discover solutions through painful trial-and-error

**Example pattern**: Person with back pain -> tries clinic (fails) -> tries another doctor (fails) -> tries manual therapy (fails) -> tries yoga specialist (works). Activating knowledge could short-circuit this: "Back pain in the neck can be caused by a pinch in the foot. Specialist X knows this." The person skips 3-4 failed attempts.

### Critical Sequence and Sequential Solution Switching
Often, a "sequential job" sequence is actually a person SWITCHING SOLUTIONS through their consideration set. They try Solution A, it fails, they try Solution B, etc.

This is an opportunity: if your activating knowledge communicates WHY other solutions fail and WHY yours succeeds at the Big Job level, you can "fire" competing solutions before the person even tries them.

## Decision Criteria for Choosing Segments

### Criterion 1: Job Frequency
Prefer segments where the target job is executed MORE FREQUENTLY. Higher frequency = more recurring value = better unit economics for subscription/cohort models.

### Criterion 2: Spending Behavior (Past Action)
Only consider people who HAVE SPENT time, money, or energy on this job in the past. Stated intent is not evidence.

### Criterion 3: Problem Severity
Prefer segments where people experience MORE PAIN with current solutions (more tax jobs, more barriers, lower satisfaction). Higher pain = higher willingness to switch.

### Criterion 4: Viral Potential
Prefer segments with MORE viral jobs executed MORE FREQUENTLY. Each viral job is a free acquisition channel.

### Criterion 5: Margin Potential
Prefer segments where the value you create justifies higher pricing. Evaluate using ABCD segmentation framework -- focus on A and B segments.

### Criterion 6: Big Job Stability
Prefer segments where the Big Job is STABLE over time (not a one-time event). Stable Big Jobs = stable demand = predictable business.

### Criterion 7: Consideration Set Accessibility
Evaluate how hard it is to enter the segment's consideration set. If no one knows your solution type exists, you need an activating knowledge delivery channel. If competing solutions are deeply entrenched (high habit, high switching cost), acquisition will be expensive.

### Criterion 8: Critical Sequence Length
Prefer segments with SHORTER critical sequences to value. Fewer steps = less drop-off = higher conversion from first contact to value delivery.

## Step-by-Step Segmentation Process

### Phase 1: Hypothesis Generation
1. Define your business task (what metric/outcome to improve)
2. Look up applicable mechanics from the mechanics table
3. Generate initial segment hypotheses based on mechanics directions

### Phase 2: Qualitative Research
1. Find 8-15 people who executed the target job in the past with some solution
2. Use the interview guide (B2C or B2B version) with appropriate modules
3. Start with Navigation block: quickly map jobs (10 min per interview)
4. Deep-dive into 2-3 most relevant jobs per interview
5. Study the job graph: up (higher-level jobs), down (lower-level jobs), backward (previous solutions), forward (next steps)
6. Record: jobs, solutions hired, satisfaction, problems, tax jobs, consideration set, activating knowledge, barriers, drivers

### Phase 3: Pattern Recognition and Segmentation
1. Cluster interviews by similar job patterns (same Big Jobs, same solution chains, same pain points)
2. Define segments by job combinations, not demographics
3. For each segment, document: Core Job, Big Job, key lower-level jobs, current solutions, satisfaction levels, main tax jobs
4. Evaluate each segment against the 8 decision criteria above

### Phase 4: Segment Selection
1. Score each segment across criteria (use RICE or similar framework)
2. Select the segment where you can create the MOST value relative to existing solutions
3. Validate: does this segment align with your business task mechanics?

### Phase 5: Validation
1. Conduct solution interviews with the selected segment
2. Test: will they buy our value proposition for their Big Job?
3. Goal: understand match/no match -- not "sell at any cost"
4. If no match: pivot (change risky assumptions) and return to Phase 2
5. If match: proceed to development investment

### Phase 6: Ongoing Maintenance
1. Refresh segmentation every 1-1.5 years (B2C mass) or as needed
2. Monitor for external events that might shift segments
3. Track ABCD distribution of current customers
4. Re-run research when segments show signs of changing

## How Business Tasks Map to Graph Pieces

| Business Task | What to Study in the Graph |
|--------------|---------------------------|
| Find segment | Big Jobs, Core Jobs, which segments exist by job combinations |
| Create value | Full lower-level job graph for target segment; identify jobs to kill |
| Grow value | Current graph vs. competitors' graphs; find jobs where we can deliver more value |
| Grow conversion | Critical sequence from first contact to value; find and fix drop-off points |
| Grow retention | First jobs after hiring our solution; first-use experience; path to Aha-moment |
| Grow average check | Higher-level jobs (can we serve them?); deeper needs; premium segments |
| Grow return rate | Frequency jobs within the graph; what triggers repeat execution |
| Reduce churn | Tax jobs our solution creates; where people drop off and why |
| Get acquisition channel | Viral jobs in the graph; orientation jobs (where do people learn about solutions?) |
| Exit direct competition | Core Job and Big Job -- redefine which job you actually compete for |

## Anti-patterns in Segmentation

### Anti-pattern: Demographic Segmentation
**What**: Segmenting by age, gender, income, location.
**Why wrong**: Demographics don't predict behavior. Two 35-year-old men with similar income may have completely different jobs.
**Instead**: Segment by job combinations and past behavior.

### Anti-pattern: Segmenting by Stated Intent
**What**: Asking "Would you use product X?" and segmenting by yes/no.
**Why wrong**: People lie to themselves and others about future behavior. These are false jobs.
**Instead**: Only segment by PAST ACTION (spent time, money, energy).

### Anti-pattern: One Segment for All
**What**: Treating all customers as one homogeneous group.
**Why wrong**: Different people hire your product for different jobs, have different satisfaction levels, and respond to different value propositions.
**Instead**: Identify distinct job-based segments. Prioritize A/B segments.

### Anti-pattern: Skipping the Business Task
**What**: Starting research without a clear business task.
**Why wrong**: Without knowing WHAT you're solving, you don't know WHERE in the graph to look. Research becomes unfocused and wasteful.
**Instead**: Always start with: "What business outcome do I need? What metrics must improve?"

### Anti-pattern: Researching Without Mechanics
**What**: Conducting interviews and hoping insights emerge organically.
**Why wrong**: The job graph is vast. Without mechanics directing your attention, you'll study irrelevant pieces.
**Instead**: Business task -> mechanics -> directed research -> apply mechanics -> hypotheses -> rank.

## Additional Knowledge from Workshop 2 (w02)

### Subsegments: The Growth Mechanism Within a Segment

A segment can be divided into subsegments -- smaller groups within the same segment that share specific job combinations or qualifying factor patterns.

**Growth loop through subsegments**:
1. Find and prioritize subsegments within the current segment
2. Select the most attractive subsegment
3. Create value for that subsegment, refine acquisition
4. Acquire customers from that subsegment
5. GOTO step 1

**Key insight**: Growth is NOT about finding entirely new segments first. Growth = creating value and acquiring customers FROM a segment/subsegment. You grow by systematically discovering and serving subsegments within your chosen segment, THEN expanding to adjacent segments.

**Example -- Revenue growth**: Initial segment generates 200M RUB. After discovering and adding a second subsegment within the same Big Job, total grows to 2B RUB. The subsegment was always there -- it was invisible because it wasn't studied separately.

### Segment Characteristics = "When" Conditions (Not Demographics)

When describing a segment, the primary descriptor is the "when" -- the contextual conditions under which a person has the target job.

**Format**: "When [I am in situation X] AND [condition Y] AND [condition Z], I want [Big Job description with expected result] [otherwise qualifier]."

**Example -- Personal coaching product for leaving employment**:
- When: "I am a top specialist OR solid expert in business [marketing, crisis management, corporate coaching, law, HR...]"
- AND: "I am tired of being employed"
- AND: "I am not doing what I want to do"
- AND: "I am accustomed to a lifestyle I am not ready to give up"
- Want (Big Job): "Leave employment while maintaining my income level, faster than 6 months"
- Otherwise: "[otherwise I can do it myself]" -- this qualifier defines the competitive threshold

**Procedural rule**: Demographics (age, gender, income) may appear as QUALIFYING FACTORS alongside job conditions, but they are never the primary segmentation axis. The job and context always come first.

### Qualifying Factors Ordering in Segmentation

When building segmentation criteria, the ORDER of qualifying factors matters. Different orderings produce different strategic views of the market.

**Example**: For a hotel product (Hotbot.ai):
- Order A: [Job: find hotel] -> [Budget: luxury] -> [Location: Europe] -> yields one strategic view
- Order B: [Job: find hotel] -> [Location: Europe] -> [Budget: luxury] -> yields a different strategic view
- Order C: [Budget: luxury] -> [Job: find hotel] -> [Location: Europe] -> yet another view

**Rule**: Jobs must ALWAYS be the first criterion in the ordering. Removing jobs from segmentation criteria is a critical error. After jobs, the ordering of demographic/behavioral qualifiers shapes the resulting segments.

### "Not-Segments" (Deliberate Segment Refusal)

**Definition**: A not-segment is a group of people you DELIBERATELY choose not to serve.

**Why it matters**: You cannot build a product for everyone. Trying to serve all segments equally leads to mediocrity for all (Porter's principle adapted for JTBD). Explicitly identifying not-segments is as important as identifying target segments.

**Procedure for defining not-segments**:
1. After identifying all potential segments, evaluate each against your 8 decision criteria
2. Segments scoring low on margin, frequency, or fit become not-segment candidates
3. Explicitly document WHY each not-segment is refused
4. Communicate not-segments to the team to prevent scope creep

**Strategic benefit**: Not-segments free resources to focus on A/B segments. They prevent the product from being diluted by low-value customers (C/D segments from ABCD framework).

### Segment Discovery Process: The Four-Quadrant Visualization

When discovering segments for the first time (before any prior segmentation), the process reveals segments with different characteristics:

**What you discover for each segment**:
- Satisfaction level (how well current solutions serve the segment's jobs)
- Margin/spending level (how much money the segment spends on the job)
- Relative size (what percentage of total addressable market this segment represents)

**Example visualization** (from Workshop 2 slides): Four segments discovered:
- Segment 1: Low satisfaction, medium spending, 15% of market
- Segment 2: Very low satisfaction, high spending, 22% of market -- **FOCUS HERE** (high pain + high spending = highest opportunity)
- Segment 3: High satisfaction, low spending, 38% of market -- deprioritize (already satisfied, low margin)
- Segment 4: Medium satisfaction, medium spending, 25% of market

**Selection heuristic**: Focus on the segment with the COMBINATION of highest dissatisfaction (most pain with current solutions) AND highest margin/spending potential. This is where you can create the most value AND capture the most revenue.

### Critical Sequences Differ by Segment and Subsegment

The same Big Job can have completely different critical sequences depending on which segment/subsegment is executing it.

**Example -- Figma users** (Big Job: "Develop new app functionality"):
- Subsegment "Solo team, ship fast": Draw mockup -> Hand off to development (skip all middle steps)
- Subsegment "Team with manager, no UX culture": Draw mockup -> Refine by manager feedback -> Hand off to development
- Subsegment "Portfolio designer": Draw mockup -> UX test prototype -> Refine by feedback (never hands off to development)

**Implication for product strategy**: When evaluating a new subsegment for expansion, first check whether your product already executes the Core Jobs in that subsegment's critical sequence. If yes, expansion is low-cost. If no, you need to build new capabilities first.

### Two Strategic Priorities for Product Growth

**Priority 1 -- Growing metrics within current segment**: Execute Core Jobs MORE EFFECTIVELY in the critical sequences of your focus segments and subsegments.

**Priority 2 -- Scaling to new segments/subsegments**: First priority for scaling is to find segments where your product can ALREADY execute Core Jobs in their critical sequences (low-effort expansion). This means looking for segments whose critical sequences overlap with what you already do.

**Critical refusal heuristic**: A potential customer may REFUSE your solution even if it delivers value, IF they know that OTHER jobs in the critical sequence (which your product does not cover) will not get executed. They will switch to an alternative solution for the Big Job -- one whose critical sequence they CAN complete, even if the result quality is lower.

### The Value Creation Starting Point

A principle stated emphatically in Workshop 2: "Solving ALL business tasks of a product must START with focus on the jobs of the target segment."

**Corollary**: "If you don't know what value to create -- study the Big Jobs of your customers in detail." Big Jobs reveal what the person is truly trying to accomplish, which illuminates where you can create the most value.

### Anti-pattern: Looking Only at Current Buyers

**What**: Studying only people who already bought your product to understand segments.
**Why wrong**: Your current buyers are a biased sample. You miss segments that (a) don't know your solution exists, (b) know it exists but chose a competitor, (c) attempted to buy but dropped off the critical sequence.
**Instead**: Study people who executed the Big Job with ANY solution in the past. Include non-buyers, competitor users, and people who tried and failed. This reveals the full landscape of segments.
