## Core Definition: What Value Is (and Is Not)

### Value is NOT:
- A feature (feature is a delivery vehicle for value, not value itself)
- Simply fixing a problem
- Making the product cheaper
- An abstract "useful thing"
- The same as "benefit" (benefit is raw; value is relative)

### Value IS:
**More efficient execution of a job compared to the client's expectations and existing solutions.**

Formula: `Value = Benefit - Costs` for a specific pair of (job + solution).

Value is always evaluated:
1. Relative to a specific job (not in abstract)
2. Relative to a specific solution hired for that job
3. Relative to the client's expectations (predictions) of how the job would be performed
4. Relative to competing solutions the client is aware of

### The Investment Principle
The brain operates as an investor. It invests current resources (glucose, oxygen, fats, dopamine, serotonin, attention, time, money) to receive greater resources in the future. Every purchase decision is evaluated through this lens: "Will the return exceed my investment?"

This drives all competition: companies compete by offering increasingly better investment deals — more return for less cost.

## The Four Brain Computation Processes Behind Purchase Decisions

### Process 1: Brain as Investor
The brain manages the body's resource balance and seeks to invest resources now to gain more resources later. Products are evaluated as investment propositions: "How much do I spend vs. how much do I gain?"

**Application**: When designing value, frame your product as a better investment deal than alternatives. Not just "we solve your problem" but "you invest X and get Y return, which is better than any alternative."

### Process 2: Seek Positive, Avoid Negative Emotions
The brain automatically, without conscious control, repeats situations that produce positive emotions (joy, calm, satisfaction, confidence) and avoids situations that produce negative emotions (anxiety, irritation, anger, sadness, disgust).

**Application**: If a client experiences negative emotions with your product, the brain will try to avoid repeating that. If a competitor's product delivers more positive emotions in the same job context, the brain will gravitate toward the competitor.

### Process 3: Dopamine — Universal Motivation Currency
Every single action is preceded by a dopamine spike relative to baseline. Dopamine drives motivation, movement toward goals, and reward evaluation. The less predictable the reward, the stronger the dopamine spike.

**Application**: Design product experiences that create anticipation and deliver rewards. Do NOT abuse dopamine loops (TikTok, gambling patterns) — use this knowledge to reinforce genuinely beneficial behaviors.

### Process 4: Reward vs. Pain Evaluation
When the brain sees a product offer, nucleus accumbens activates (reward anticipation). When shown a price, the insula activates (pain/cost evaluation). The medial prefrontal cortex then compares: "Is expected reward greater than expected pain?" If yes — buy. If no — don't.

**Application**: Increase perceived reward AND reduce perceived pain simultaneously. The purchase decision is literally reward minus pain.

## The Four Forces of Progress (Switching Behavior)

When a person considers switching from current solution to a new one, four forces operate:

### Forces driving switching (toward the new solution):
1. **Push of the current situation** — Dissatisfaction, problems, frustrations with the current way of doing the job. The negative emotional signal: "Something is wrong with how I do this now."
2. **Pull of the new solution** — Attractiveness of the new product. The positive emotional signal: "This looks like it would be much better." Expectation of reward.

### Forces resisting switching (keeping the status quo):
3. **Anxiety about the new solution** — Fear that the new thing won't work, will be worse, will cost hidden resources. "What if this doesn't work? What if I lose what I have?"
4. **Habit of the current situation** — Comfort of the familiar. Sunk costs. Existing workflows, integrations, relationships. "I know how this works. It's not great, but it's mine."

### Decision Framework: For Switching to Happen
`Push + Pull > Anxiety + Habit`

**Procedure for applying the four forces:**
1. Identify the specific push — what exactly is wrong with the current solution? Interview using "What's hard about that?" (chain 5 times minimum)
2. Identify the specific pull — what about your solution excites them? What do they anticipate?
3. Identify specific anxieties — what are they afraid of? What do they imagine going wrong?
4. Identify specific habits — what routines would they have to break? What sunk costs exist?
5. Design your product to maximize push awareness (help them see their real problems), maximize pull (demonstrate clear value), minimize anxiety (guarantees, trials, onboarding), and minimize habit friction (migration tools, compatibility)

## Prediction Error: The Engine of Problem and Delight

### How the Brain Evaluates Value
The brain is a prediction machine. It automatically and unconsciously predicts the entire job graph — expected costs, expected benefit, expected effort, expected time. Then it compares prediction with reality.

### Three Outcomes:

**Negative prediction error** (reality worse than expected) = PROBLEM
- Expected to spend 1000 rubles, spent 1500
- Expected pleasant service, got rudeness
- Expected 1 hour, took all day
- Produces negative emotions: frustration, anger, disappointment

**Match** (reality meets expectations) = BASELINE QUALITY
- Expected experience matches actual experience
- No strong emotional reaction — "it's okay, it's normal"
- This is the minimum to not lose customers

**Positive prediction error** (reality better than expected) = DELIGHT / WOW
- Expected 1000 rubles, paid 900, got better service, plus a candy
- Produces positive emotions: surprise, joy, gratitude

### Critical Insight: Hedonic Adaptation
What was "wow" yesterday becomes expected today. The prediction model constantly relearns.
- Tinkoff Bank: delivering cards by courier was amazing in 2015 → expected by 2020
- Expectation baseline constantly rises due to competitor improvements and general market evolution

**Procedure when designing value:**
1. Research current expectations of your target segment for the specific job
2. Know that different segments have different expectations (Yandex team vs. a no-experience client)
3. Design to exceed expectations, not just meet them
4. Plan for hedonic adaptation — your "wow" has a shelf life
5. Consider actively correcting unrealistic expectations downward to enable delight

## Expected Result Criteria

Different people with different incomes, standards, and contexts expect different qualities of the same job result. Value lives not in "what job" but in "how the job should be done."

### Example: Yandex Taxi Evolution
Same job: get from point A to point B.
- Economy: cheaply, fast
- Comfort: clean car, no smell, pleasant ride
- Comfort+: Toyota Camry, nice scent
- Business: silence, luxury, door opened for you
- Ultima: arrive like royalty

### Interview Questions to Uncover Criteria:
- "How did you want to get the result?"
- "What was most important to you when getting this result?"
- "By what criteria did you evaluate whether the result was successful?"
- "What does [quality word they used] mean to you? Give me an example."

### Anti-pattern: Ignoring Criteria
If you know the job but not the criteria, you may build a solution that technically does the job but fails on the "how" — and the client won't buy.

## 17 Mechanics of Value Creation

### Mechanic 1: Fix problems (negative prediction errors)
**When**: The current solution causes pain, frustration, or unexpected costs.
**How**: Identify specific problems via interview ("What's hard about that?" x5). Remove or reduce the cause.
**Note**: Most common and most obvious mechanic. Everyone does it. Necessary but insufficient for differentiation.

### Mechanic 2: Make it cheaper (reduce monetary cost)
**When**: The current solution is overpriced relative to the value delivered.
**How**: Reduce price, change pricing model, remove unnecessary cost components.
**Warning**: Competing on price alone is a race to the bottom. Damages margins. Should be combined with other mechanics.

### Mechanic 3: Kill a job entirely
**When**: A sub-job in the critical sequence adds cost but no direct benefit to the user.
**How**: Remove the need for that step entirely. The job ceases to exist as a class.
**Examples**:
- Uber killed "learn to drive" and "find parking" jobs
- Cloud services killed "configure server" job for most users
- Zoom killed "drive to meeting location" job
- Digital photography killed "develop film" job

### Mechanic 4: Perform more jobs with one solution (super-app)
**When**: Client currently uses multiple solutions for related jobs.
**How**: Consolidate multiple jobs into one product. Reduce context-switching costs.
**Example**: WeChat in China — messaging, payments, taxi, food delivery, all in one.

### Mechanic 5: Perform the next job in the critical sequence
**When**: After the client finishes their current job with your product, they need to do something else.
**How**: Extend your product to cover the next step in their workflow.
**Example**: Avito added delivery — after choosing an item, you no longer need to "go pick it up yourself."

### Mechanic 6: Go to the PREVIOUS job (upstream)
**When**: Before the client reaches your product's core job, they have orientation/research/preparation jobs.
**How**: Create content, tools, or services for the step before your core job.
**Examples**:
- Aviasales: before "buy a ticket" comes "find the cheapest option"
- DomClick (Sberbank): before "get a mortgage" comes "choose a district, estimate costs, find an apartment"
- Movehub: before "hire movers" comes "understand options and costs"
**Strategic power**: This is the most accessible mechanic for escaping direct competition AND creating acquisition channels.

### Mechanic 7: Go to the HIGHER-LEVEL job (accelerated job)
**When**: Your core job is actually a sub-job of a bigger job the client cares about.
**How**: Take ownership of the entire bigger job. Kill all sub-jobs below your new scope.
**Examples**:
- Rolls-Royce: from "sell engines" to "guarantee flight hours" — client no longer manages maintenance
- Meal delivery (not ingredients): "just eat" — no shopping, cooking, cleaning
- ChatGPT: from "search + read + synthesize" to "get an answer"
**Note**: This is the disruptive innovation mechanic Christensen tried to describe. Applied at high job levels, it becomes a market-creation strategy.

### Mechanic 8: Correct expectations
**When**: Client expectations are unrealistically high (leading to inevitable disappointment) or unrealistically low (leading to missed sales).
**How**: Explicitly set realistic expectations before the experience.
**Example**: At practice sessions, telling students "You will learn to conduct minimally adequate interviews, NOT become a master" — then 90%+ students are satisfied.

### Mechanic 9: Perform the job faster
**When**: Time-to-result is a critical criterion for the segment.
**How**: Reduce waiting, processing, or delivery time.

### Mechanic 10: Perform the job with more certainty/guarantee
**When**: Uncertainty about the outcome is a major anxiety.
**How**: Add guarantees, SLAs, money-back promises, transparent tracking.

### Mechanic 11: Perform the job with higher quality
**When**: Quality of result is the differentiating criterion.
**How**: Invest in output quality beyond what competitors offer.

### Mechanic 12: Reduce cognitive load
**When**: The job requires significant mental effort, decision-making, or learning.
**How**: Simplify interfaces, provide defaults, offer recommendations, reduce choice.

### Mechanic 13: Reduce emotional load
**When**: The job is emotionally taxing (fear, anxiety, embarrassment).
**How**: Add emotional support, normalize the experience, reduce social friction.

### Mechanic 14: Activate value (load value into the client's head)
**When**: You created value but the client doesn't know about it.
**How**: Communicate through the chain: Big Job -> Core Job -> How much more efficiently -> Through what features. The client is not a telepath.
**Anti-pattern**: Building great value but writing a bad landing page, giving dull demos, failing to articulate the job-value-feature chain.

### Mechanic 15: Deliver status
**When**: The client's job has a social/status dimension.
**How**: Add visible markers of achievement, social proof, premium tiers.
**Note**: Status is a deep evolutionary need — the brain constantly and unconsciously tracks hierarchical position. Status products (luxury, premium) have enormous margins.

### Mechanic 16: Deliver positive emotions beyond functional result
**When**: Functional value is commoditized, all competitors do it equally well.
**How**: Design for delight, aesthetics, surprise, community, belonging.

### Mechanic 17: Reduce barriers to starting
**When**: The client is interested but doesn't start because of onboarding friction.
**How**: Free trials, freemium, simplified registration, migration assistance.

## Anti-patterns in Value Creation

### Anti-pattern: "Better Mousetrap"
**What**: Building a superior product and assuming people will come.
**Why wrong**: Value must be activated — loaded into the client's brain. You must communicate the job-value-feature chain. A better product with no communication is invisible.
**Instead**: Budget equal effort for creating value AND communicating value.

### Anti-pattern: Feature-First Thinking
**What**: Starting with "let's build this feature" instead of "what job does this serve?"
**Why wrong**: Leads to the "cursed cycle of feature shipping" — build, launch, discover nobody needs it, support it forever. Features are the last link in the chain: Job -> Value -> Feature.
**Instead**: Always start from the job. Ask "what job does this feature serve? For what segment? How does it make the job more efficient?"

### Anti-pattern: Price Competition as Primary Strategy
**What**: Defaulting to "we'll just be cheaper."
**Why wrong**: One-way path that degrades economics. Competitors follow. No sustainable advantage. 15 other value mechanics exist.
**Instead**: Use price as ONE mechanic alongside others. Identify which non-price mechanics create the most delta for your target segment.

### Anti-pattern: Ignoring Segment Differences in Expectations
**What**: Building one experience for all segments.
**Why wrong**: The Yandex engineering team and a first-time client have wildly different expectations. The same product can be "wow" for one and "terrible" for another.
**Instead**: Know your segments' expectations. Either focus on segments where your current quality is "wow" or invest in quality for high-expectation segments.

### Anti-pattern: Ignoring Hedonic Adaptation
**What**: Resting on a "wow" feature from 2 years ago.
**Why wrong**: Prediction models retrain. Today's delight is tomorrow's expectation. Competitors raise the bar constantly.
**Instead**: Plan for continuous value evolution. Monitor competitor improvements. Track when your "wow" becomes "expected."

### Anti-pattern: Creating Value Without Activating It
**What**: Building a great product but failing to communicate its value.
**Why wrong**: "Clients are not telepaths." If the landing page, demo, or sales pitch doesn't articulate jobs, value, and features in sequence, the client won't understand why your product is better.
**Instead**: Use the communication formula: "We will perform [core jobs] for [big jobs] this much more efficiently, using [features]."

## Connection Between Jobs and Value

Value does not exist in isolation. It is always a transformation of the job graph:

1. **Job** defines what the person is trying to accomplish
2. **Current solution** defines how they currently accomplish it (with current costs and benefits)
3. **Expectations** define what the person predicts about costs and benefits
4. **Value** = how much more efficiently your solution performs the job relative to expectations and current solutions
5. **Features** = the specific product elements through which value is delivered

The causal chain is: `Needs -> Goals -> Jobs -> Value -> Features`

Never start from features. Never start from value in abstract. Start from the job, understand the segment's expectations, then design value as job graph transformation, then determine what features deliver that value.

## The Role of Emotions in Purchase Decisions

### Key Findings:
- At the bottom of every problem lies an emotion (discovered through "What's hard about that?" x5 technique)
- At the bottom of every valued product feature lies an emotion (discovered through "What's great about that?" x5 technique)
- 90% of daily decisions are made unconsciously, on autopilot
- The "inner narrator" (left hemisphere) constructs post-hoc rationalizations for emotional decisions
- When people say why they bought something, they often construct plausible but false narratives

### Practical Implication:
Do NOT take customer statements at face value about WHY they bought. Dig deeper with emotional laddering. The real drivers are emotional and often unconscious. Your product must satisfy both functional AND emotional jobs.

### Status as Emotional Driver:
Status is an evolutionary mechanism — the brain constantly tracks "Where am I in the hierarchy? Am I moving up or down?" This happens automatically, unconsciously, for everyone. Products that raise perceived status (luxury brands, premium tiers, visible achievements) tap into one of the deepest human drives.

## Decision Framework: Choosing Value Mechanics

1. **Map the job graph** for your target segment's core job
2. **Identify the current solution** and its costs/benefits profile
3. **Research expectations** — what does the segment predict about how this job should go?
4. **Scan all 17 mechanics** — which ones create the largest delta between your solution and expectations?
5. **Prioritize by delta size** — the mechanic that creates the biggest positive prediction error wins
6. **Verify feasibility** — can you actually deliver this with your resources and competencies?
7. **Plan activation** — how will you load this value into the client's head?
8. **Plan for hedonic adaptation** — what's your next value move when this one becomes expected?
