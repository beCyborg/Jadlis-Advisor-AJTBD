## Key Processes

### Interview Preparation Process

**When**: Before every research interview (JTBD segment search, product research, or sales/communication research).

**Steps**:

1. **Define the research goal first** — never conduct interviews without a goal. Interview without a goal can lead to false segments, wrong jobs, or irrelevant insights. Design the research: who to interview, what to ask, how to interpret.
2. **Do desk research on the domain** — read articles, watch videos, learn the terminology of the field. When interviewing prosthetics users, learn what traction vs. bionic prosthetics are. When they say "I switched from traction to bionic," you understand immediately.
3. **Prepare the interview guide** — use logical blocks (qualifying block, job navigation block, deep study block). Do NOT memorize questions — internalize the STRUCTURE so you can navigate flexibly during the conversation.
4. **Set the duration** — optimal: 30-90 minutes. Under 30 min = not enough time for emotional contact + substantive questions. Over 90 min = fatigue destroys attention for both parties. Aim for ~60 min as the sweet spot.
5. **Prepare the recording setup** — decide on audio vs. video, check storage space (bitter lesson: lost an expert interview due to full storage). Use Zoom/Google Meet with video, not phone calls.
6. **Prepare the note-taking space** — create a dedicated page (Notion, board, etc.) with the respondent's name, all questions from the guide, and space for answers next to each question. This keeps the guide visible during the interview and makes notes organizable afterward.
7. **Prepare yourself** — find 5-10 minutes before the interview to reset. Meditate, make tea, look out the window. Remove all distractions: phone notifications, email, computer alerts. Open the door for the cat.

**Notes**: Do NOT conduct interviews by phone. 55%+ of information travels through non-verbal channels (gestures, facial expressions, gaze). Phone calls are also associated with negative contexts (spam, delivery issues). If the respondent insists on phone, try to convert to video during the call.

### Interview Execution: The Four-Phase Structure

**When**: During any deep interview, especially JTBD interviews.

#### Phase 1: Boundaries + Emotional Contact (first 3-5 minutes)

Simultaneously set boundaries AND establish emotional contact. Interleave formal framing with casual warmth.

**Steps**:
1. Greet warmly: "Hi, thank you for finding time for our interview."
2. Ask if "ty" (informal address) is comfortable — "ty" = two equals having a human conversation; "vy" (formal) = two roles at a distance.
3. Explain what you'll talk about: "I'd like to learn about how you [topic]." In B2C: do NOT reveal your product or idea to avoid framing the respondent.
4. Confirm the time: "We agreed on 45 minutes, I'll watch the time."
5. Ask permission to take notes: "Is it OK if I type during our conversation? I want to capture everything you say. I'll only share with my direct colleagues."
6. Ask permission to record: "Is it OK if we record? Only my team will see it." B2B respondents refuse recording more often than B2C due to NDA sensitivity.
7. Normalize emotional questions: "Some questions may seem strange — I'll ask things like 'how did you want to feel?' This is because we're all different and my emotional experience is different from yours."
8. Demonstrate emotions yourself to set the norm: "It drives me crazy when I explain something to employees and they still don't remember, and I have to deal with the consequences." This shows vulnerability and signals that emotional honesty is safe.
9. Give permission to skip: "If you don't want to answer any question, just tell me, that's totally fine."
10. Establish rapport through small talk, smiling, open body language, eye contact.

**Critical Rule**: Mix formal boundary-setting with casual warmth. Do NOT do all boundaries first then try to establish contact — interleave them. Example flow: "Hi, I'm Vanya, how are you feeling? → I'm very interested in your experience with X → By the way, is it OK if I record? → What do you think about [recent news]?"

#### Phase 2: Qualifying Block (2 min to half the interview)

**Steps**:
1. Start with simple, safe questions: "Where do you live? What do you do? What are your hobbies?" — continues the safe environment while revealing the person's thinking, life context, personality.
2. In JTBD interviews, ask qualifying questions to identify segments. When searching for segments and you don't understand them well — this block can take up to HALF the interview time.
3. Once the segment is found, this block collapses to 1-3 filtering questions.

#### Phase 3: Main Question Block

**Steps**:
1. Navigate into jobs: ask about PAST experience, never future. "Tell me the story of how you previously [did X]. What solutions did you use?"
2. Study the job graph: core jobs, micro-jobs, big jobs, triggers, context.
3. Study value: what was valuable about the solutions they hired? What were the expected outcomes?
4. Study problems: what was difficult? Dig into emotions with the "What is hard about..." technique (see below).
5. Study barriers: what fears or doubts did they have?

#### Phase 4: Closing

**Steps**:
1. Ask for referrals: "Do you know anyone who also [qualification criterion]?" — about half of respondents agree to recommend. Deeply depends on how strong the emotional contact was.
2. Ask the golden closing question: "Is there anything important that I didn't ask you?" — You spent an hour training the respondent's neural network about what interests you. Ask this network directly. One-third of respondents reveal something extremely valuable that your script didn't cover.

### The "What Is Hard About..." Technique

**When**: During the problem study block, to dig from surface problems to root emotions.

**Steps**:

1. Respondent states a problem: "When I go to workouts with my friend, I have to constantly adjust to her schedule."
2. Ask: "What is hard about the fact that you have to constantly adjust to her schedule?" (QUOTE their exact words after "what is hard about the fact that...")
3. Respondent answers: "I end up exercising early morning or late evening."
4. Ask again: "What is hard about the fact that you exercise early morning or late evening?"
5. Respondent reveals emotion: "On workout days I can't sleep properly, everything irritates me."
6. You hear the emotion (irritation). Ask to rate it: "On a scale of 1 to 10, where 10 is you want to destroy everything around you — how strong is this irritation?"
7. Always verbalize what 10 means on your scale so the respondent calibrates properly.

**Critical Error to Avoid**: Do NOT say "What's so hard about adjusting to your friend's schedule?" — this sounds dismissive, as if you're saying "what's the big deal?" Instead, ALWAYS quote: "What is hard about the fact that [exact quote]?" The quoting validates the respondent's experience instead of minimizing it.

**Notes**: This technique may feel awkward at first. Push through the discomfort — it reveals enormously valuable emotional data. Without it, you get surface-level "it's fine" responses.

### Establishing Emotional Contact: The Three-Gate Model

**When**: At the start of any interview or meeting where you need the other person to open up.

**Framework**: The human brain's limbic system and brain stem automatically evaluate three questions about any new person. Until all three are answered positively, the person remains guarded.

**Gate 1: "Are you a threat to me?"**
- Processed in milliseconds by the reptilian brain
- Triggered by: aggressive posture, fast/pressured speech, standing over someone, looking like a salesperson, closed body language
- **How to pass**: Slow down. Smile. Open hands (no weapons — literally wired into DNA from millions of years of ancestors being killed). Do not stand over people. Do not invade personal space aggressively.
- Time to pass: 1-5 minutes if done well, seconds if done perfectly.

**Gate 2: "Where are you in the social hierarchy relative to me?"**
- Processed by the same ancient neural circuits found in lobsters (200+ million years old, per Jordan Peterson's research)
- When you hunch/hide/curl up → you drop in perceived hierarchy → the other person finds you uninteresting
- When you stand straight with open posture → equal level → conversation becomes worthwhile
- **How to pass**: Stand/sit straight. Open posture. Match the respondent's energy level. Do not over-dominate (arms behind back = power position = danger for them) and do not cower.

**Gate 3: "Are you part of my tribe? Do we share something?"**
- If gates 1 and 2 pass but you share nothing → no motivation to communicate
- **How to pass**: Find common ground. Demonstrate shared experiences. Share your own relevant emotional experiences.

**After all three gates**: Basic emotional contact is established. Person answers factual questions. But they do NOT yet share emotions proactively.

**To reach DEEP emotional contact** (where people proactively share emotions, tell you things you didn't ask, become "buddies"):
- Demonstrate vulnerability: share your own emotional stories relevant to the topic
- Use the smile-mirror-neuron mechanism (see below)
- Give it time — but use techniques to compress the usual social intimacy timeline

### Mirror Neurons and the Smile Mechanism

**When**: Throughout the entire interview, but especially in the first minutes.

**How it works**:
1. You smile → the respondent's mirror neurons fire → their motor cortex activates the same facial muscles → they start smiling → their emotional center activates → they feel the same positive emotions you project.
2. Smile = the foundational signal between mother and infant: "Everything is OK, everything is safe, don't worry." This wiring persists into adulthood. When you smile at someone, their brain unconsciously tags you as "safe/maternal" and releases oxytocin (same amount as 40 seconds of hugging).
3. After age 3, mirror neurons are overridden by actual sensory feedback — but the initial emotional resonance still fires first.

**Research evidence**: Two groups of respondents were interviewed. Group 1: interviewer had arms crossed/behind back and did not smile. Group 2: interviewer had open hands, active gestures, and smiled. Group 2 respondents answered personal questions faster and answered a higher percentage of personal questions.

**Practical note**: If you don't know how to smile naturally — practice. Force it through your teeth at first. What feels like a huge smile to you probably looks barely perceptible to others. Have your interview partner hold up a "SMILE" sign during the interview.

### Demonstrating Vulnerability (The Pratfall Effect)

**When**: Early in the interview to accelerate trust-building.

**Mechanism**: Share your own emotional experience related to the topic. This demonstrates vulnerability, which — for a person of perceived equal or higher status — INCREASES trust.

**Example** (Pasha Kozlov, Yandex.Music): After three interviews where respondents claimed they only watch TED talks on YouTube, Pasha started saying: "I want to understand what people REALLY watch on YouTube. When I come home dead tired from work — unboxing videos, cats, Dud, Dota. And when it's really bad — Fashion TV, half-naked models on the runway, brain turns to jelly. What about you?" After this, respondents authorized their YouTube accounts and showed the truth: same stuff — Dota, cats, unboxing.

**Research backing** (school cafeteria study): When a "normal" student drops their tray in the cafeteria, their reputation stays the same but trust toward them INCREASES. When a "nerd" drops their tray, both reputation and trust DECREASE. If you have baseline credibility (passed Gate 2), demonstrating a flaw increases trust. If you don't — it hurts you.

**Anti-pattern**: Do NOT fake vulnerability. It must be genuine. People detect inauthenticity.

**Physical vulnerability**: Drop a pen, spill something. If it happens accidentally — don't try to hide it. People unconsciously relax when they see someone else make a mistake, because "I'm not the one who messed up" — and the chronic fear of making mistakes momentarily dissolves.

### Physical Contact and Space Hacking

**When**: Meeting respondents in person (less applicable to Zoom).

**Levels**:
1. **Handshake pattern break**: Instead of the automatic mindless handshake (brains don't engage), do something unexpected — fist bump, or a warm two-handed handshake. The pattern break activates attention: "Something interesting just happened."
2. **Hugging**: Works powerfully but requires reading the situation. Zamesin reports hugging ~70% of female respondents and ~25-30% of male respondents at first meeting (when the social context is collaborative). Conversations after a hug operate on a fundamentally different contact level.
3. **Eye contact + smile + closeness**: Looking into eyes while smiling and being physically close hacks the normal social intimacy timeline. The alarm ("someone is in my intimate space!") fires, but the simultaneous safety signals ("smiling, non-threatening, friendly") override it. You compress what normally takes multiple meetings into one minute.

**Cultural calibration**: Northern peoples (Finns, Estonians) — enormous personal space, houses far apart. Southern peoples (Italians) — discuss personal lives at urinals. Choose a comfortable approach for your culture and context.

### UX Test Methodology

**When**: Testing any "medium" — the intermediary through which the customer interacts with your product: mobile/desktop interfaces, landing pages, emails, sales scripts, messenger interactions, offline spaces, physical products.

**Steps**:

1. **Set the context**: Tell the respondent HOW they arrived at this point. "Your friend sent you a link. He knows you're looking for [specific board game]. You can buy it here. Please, buy it."
2. **Place at "minus-one" step**: Don't put them directly on the screen you're testing. Place them one step BEFORE — this lets you discover activation problems (person can't find the entry point to the feature you're testing).
3. **Ask the respondent to perform the job**: "You want to understand if this course is right for you" or "You want to order breakfast in this cafe."
4. **On each screen/state, ask the 5-question cycle**:
   - **"What do you see in front of you?"** — first reaction, what they read/notice
   - **"What thoughts come to your mind?"** — deeper cognitive processing, decision forks
   - **"What do you feel?"** — emotional reaction: positive and negative
   - **"What CAN you do?"** — do they see entry points to perform jobs? Do they see controls to continue? (Finds activation problems)
   - **"What action do you WANT to take?"** — the specific next action they want to perform
5. **Do NOT let them scroll or navigate** until they answer all five questions. After they state what they want to do, say "Do it!" and they move to the next screen/state.
6. **Remind them they can always close the tab**: "Remember, you can always close this tab / leave the store / delete the app." On the "what can you do?" question, ask: "Would you really go forward, or would you close the tab?" This reveals drop-off points.
7. **Always ask "Why?"** when you get information but don't fully understand the motivation.

**Duration**: 10-40 minutes per test, median ~20 minutes.

### UX Test Iteration Process (RITE Approach)

**When**: Building or improving any interface/medium iteratively.

**Steps**:

1. Create a prototype/first version
2. Conduct 4 UX tests (one iteration)
3. Collect all problems found by 2+ out of 4 respondents
4. Fix the problems
5. Conduct 4 more UX tests with NEW respondents (never reuse people from previous iterations)
6. Expect fewer problems. Fix them.
7. Repeat until: either ZERO problems where someone can't perform the job (strict school), or at most 1 problem across 4 respondents (relaxed school)
8. Optionally, confirm with one more iteration of 4 to verify

**Timeline**: Typical cycle is 2-3 days per iteration. Total cleanup usually takes 1.5-2 weeks.

**When to fix**: Fix a problem if it appeared in 2+ out of 4-5 respondents. If it appeared in only 1 person BUT you believe it's not unique — fix it too. 2 out of 5 = with 80% confidence, the problem affects 18%+ of users.

**Sample sizes**:
- 3 tests: finds problems affecting 20%+ of users (with 80% statistical power)
- 5 tests: typical practical number
- 7 tests: finds problems affecting 10%+ of users (with 80% statistical power)
- For complex interfaces with many jobs: 5-7 respondents per high-level job on final iterations

---

### Who to Test With

**When**: Selecting respondents for UX tests.

**Rules**:
- **First 1-2 iterations**: Anyone who can IMAGINE themselves performing the job. You don't need actual users yet. Exception: highly specialized B2B products (dental crown modeling software, solar panel calculation) — need real specialists from the start.
- **Later iterations**: People who actually have or had the job. First two iterations buy you 1-2 weeks to recruit real users.
- **For testing communication and sales mechanics**: Need real people who either performed the job or are ready to buy RIGHT NOW.
- **Each iteration**: New respondents only. Previous ones already know your interface.

### Writing Notes During Interviews (Not Transcribing Later)

**When**: Every interview, always.

**Why this is non-negotiable**:
1. **Almost nobody rewatches recordings** — Zamesin surveyed 2000+ product specialists; 90%+ never rewatched their interview recordings.
2. **Typical failure mode**: Conduct 7 interviews in a week without notes → plan transcription for Friday → forget → remember at 4 PM → face choice: transcribe until 11 PM or "write down what I remember" → choose the latter → lose 80% of interview value.
3. **Opportunity cost**: The time you'd spend transcribing one interview = time to conduct ANOTHER interview.
4. **Recording creates false safety**: The "recording" badge relaxes you ("I won't miss anything") → you miss weak signals → you don't ask the crucial follow-up → especially dangerous on 2nd-3rd interview of the day when cortisol drops.

**How to take notes**:
1. Type during the interview (if you type slowly — learn touch typing, many free trainers exist)
2. If interviewing in person — place laptop slightly to the side, periodically glance at it
3. If you need more time: "Give me a moment please, this is very interesting, I need to write it down." Nobody has ever been irritated by this.
4. Always warn at the start: "I'll be typing during our conversation to capture everything"
5. Immediately after the interview, spend 15-30 minutes completing your notes while memories are fresh. If you wait a week, you'll lose most of the nuance.

### Interview in Pairs (For Beginners)

**When**: You have conducted fewer than 20 interviews in your career.

**Setup**:
- **You**: talk to the respondent, establish and maintain rapport, make branching decisions about where to take the conversation
- **Your partner**: writes notes, corrects you if you go in the wrong direction. The partner does NOT talk to the respondent (otherwise they'd need to establish their own rapport = wasted time)

## Anti-patterns

### Asking About the Future
**What**: "What would you do if [hypothetical future scenario]?" or "Would you buy this product if it existed?"
**Why wrong**: People are terrible at predicting their future behavior. Fitness centers, credit companies, and casinos build their business models on this exact human failure. During an interview, the respondent WANTS to please you (social transaction) — they'll say what you want to hear about the future. JTBD insight: past energy expenditure proves the existence of a job; fantasy about the future proves nothing.
**Instead**: Always ask about PAST experience. "Tell me the story of how you previously [did X]." If you accidentally ask about the future, quickly pivot: "OK, have you been in this situation before? How did you actually handle it?"

### Asking Closed Questions Predominantly
**What**: "Did you buy the subscription because of the ad or because someone recommended it?"
**Why wrong**: You're already suggesting answers. The respondent picks one of YOUR options instead of revealing the full spectrum of actual reasons.
**Instead**: "What exactly prompted you to buy the subscription?" Open questions = the heart of deep interviews. Closed questions won't sink the interview, but strive for open questions to dominate.

### Asking Multiple Questions at Once
**What**: "What was the hardest moment when trying to call the operator? What happened in the end? How did you solve it? How did you feel?"
**Why wrong**: The brain remembers first or last elements (edge effect). The respondent answers one question, the others are lost.
**Instead**: One question at a time. Wait for the complete answer. Then ask the next.

### Filling Silences
**What**: When the respondent pauses, you immediately offer options, rephrase, suggest answers.
**Why wrong**: The person may simply need time to think or remember. Your "help" narrows their response to YOUR suggestion. They grab your hint and stop thinking.
**Instead**: Embrace pauses. Silence is a tool — it gives space for thinking. Silence can also draw out truth: when you sense the respondent is not being fully honest and you hold the pause, the discomfort may push them to reveal what they're hiding.

### Not Redirecting a Rambling Respondent
**What**: Letting the respondent talk for 15 minutes about something irrelevant to your research because you don't want to be rude.
**Why wrong**: You lose precious time and fail to get what you need. You also fail the respondent's own goal (helping you build a great product).
**Instead**: "What you're sharing is very interesting. Let me moderate a bit — we have limited time. Can we return to [topic X]?" 99% of people respond to this completely normally. Many people with focus issues are GRATEFUL for the redirection.

### Not Challenging the Respondent
**What**: Accepting all answers at face value, never pushing back on exaggerated ratings or socially desirable answers.
**Why wrong**: People lie — to look better, to please you, to earn the interview incentive. If you don't verify, you get noise instead of signal.
**Instead**: Challenge directly. "You said this is 8 out of 10 on the importance scale where 10 is life and death. Are you SURE?" Look them in the eyes. If it's genuinely important — they'll argue back passionately: "Yes, 8! I decided to build my career as a massage therapist and getting the table fast means getting feedback faster because I have so many doubts." If it's inflated — they'll correct: "Well, maybe 5."

### Revealing the Research Goal
**What**: Telling the respondent what exactly you're trying to learn.
**Why wrong**: As soon as they understand your goal, they unconsciously adjust answers to match what you want to hear. Cognitive bias: the desire to be helpful overrides truthfulness.
**Instead**: Keep the goal hidden. For JTBD interviews this is naturally easier — the respondent doesn't know what you expect beyond their story.

### Not Trying to Sell at the End of Solution Interviews
**What**: Showing the product concept but never actually trying to sell it.
**Why wrong**: You miss the most valuable signal. When you just ask "what do you think?" — you get polite feedback. When you try to SELL — the response under the pressure of real money reveals the truth: "nice but I won't buy" is fundamentally different from "nice."
**Instead**: At the end of solution interviews, attempt an actual sale. The quality of the signal depends on emotional contact level:
- No contact → refusal could mean distrust, not product irrelevance (noisy signal)
- Basic contact → still ambiguous
- Deep contact → refusal likely means they genuinely don't need it (clean signal)

### Dressing Inappropriately for the Respondent
**What**: Coming to interview call center workers in a suit, or meeting executives in jeans and flip-flops.
**Why wrong**: Clothing mismatch signals "not my tribe" (Gate 3 failure). When a bank's product team in suits interviewed support staff, everyone claimed to be super responsible. When they came back in jeans and t-shirts (matching support staff dress code), people opened up because "management isn't evaluating us anymore."
**Instead**: Dress like the respondent. Simple clothes for blue-collar workers. Modest business casual for corporate respondents.

## Frameworks

### Two Types of Empathy in Research

**Purpose**: Understand what you're cultivating through interviews.

| Type | Definition | Role in Research |
|------|-----------|-----------------|
| Cognitive empathy | Knowing what the person feels and WHY they feel it | You can articulate their jobs, triggers, and decision logic |
| Affective empathy | Actually FEELING what the other person feels | You internalize their emotional reality; enables intuitive product decisions |

Through many interviews, neural networks of key customer segments form in the product person's head. An experienced PM can say "this won't work" without new research — because the internalized customer networks quickly model the reaction. This is NOT magic — it's the result of hundreds of interviews building affective empathy through mirror neurons and right-hemisphere frequency attunement.

### UX Test vs. Usability Testing

**Purpose**: Choose the right tool for the right situation.

| Dimension | UX Test | Usability Testing |
|-----------|---------|-------------------|
| Output | Qualitative: works/doesn't work, clear/unclear, found/not found | Quantitative: time to find control, time to complete task |
| Formality | Loosely moderated, conversational | Strictly moderated, formalized |
| Best for | Early iterations, finding big problems cheaply and fast | Mature products, complex interfaces with many scenarios to optimize |
| Cost | Very cheap, very fast | Expensive, slow, requires specialized equipment |
| When to use | Building from scratch, first versions, rapid iteration | Polishing high-traffic mature interfaces |

### Emotional Contact Levels

**Purpose**: Understand what information is accessible at each level.

| Level | Respondent Behavior | What You Can Ask |
|-------|---------------------|-----------------|
| No contact | Closed posture, no eye contact, minimal answers | Nothing useful — establish basic contact first |
| Basic contact | Open, answers questions, looks at you | Factual questions. Emotional questions often fail ("How do you feel?" → "Fine.") |
| Deep contact | Proactively shares, tells stories unprompted, shares emotions without being asked, "we part as friends" | Everything: emotions, fears, embarrassing truths. Ratings become honest. Sales attempts produce clean signal. |

**Timeline to deep contact**: Depends on interviewer skill. Beginners: 20-25 minutes (leaving only 20-25 min for substance in a 45-min interview). Skilled: 5-10 minutes. Expert: first minutes.

## Examples

### YouTube Viewing Habits (Pasha Kozlov, Yandex.Music)
**Context**: Interviewing people about what they watch on YouTube. First three interviews: everyone says TED, TED, TED.
**Action**: Pasha started demonstrating vulnerability — sharing his own "guilty pleasure" viewing (unboxing, cats, Fashion TV). Respondents then authorized their YouTube accounts.
**Result**: Real viewing habits emerged — same "guilty pleasures" (Dota, cats, unboxing). Nothing like the socially desirable "TED talks."
**Lesson**: Demonstration of vulnerability normalizes honest answers. Without it, you get the "official version."

### SkySmart Communication (From Emotional Contact Workshop)
**Context**: Student on the course conducted 10 interviews with mothers with children (target segment for an ed-tech product). Interviews lasted ~2 hours each.
**Action**: Deep emotional contact was established. By the end, respondents became "friends."
**Result**: Discovered the product concept was not needed in Russia (different technology adoption level), confirming the need to target the US market. Also learned that "trying to instill new habits is nearly impossible" — validating the AJTBD principle that you cannot create new habits through products.
**Lesson**: Long interviews with deep contact reveal strategic insights (market selection, not just feature decisions). Also: discovering your product is not needed IS a valuable research outcome.

### Bank Support Team Interviews
**Context**: Product team in suits interviews call center support staff about their work quality.
**Action**: Everyone says "we work very responsibly."
**Result**: When team returns in jeans and t-shirts (matching staff dress code), staff opens up about real problems.
**Lesson**: Clothing mismatch blocks Gate 3 ("are you my tribe?"). Matching dress code removes perceived hierarchy, enabling honest responses.

### Litres E-book Store UX Test
**Context**: Testing the Litres main page with 6 users.
**Action**: Asked users to find and buy books using the 5-question UX test protocol.
**Result**: 6/6 said "I don't understand why I'm being shown these books." 5/6 said "my eyes are wandering, I don't understand anything." 4/6 disliked the visual layout and structure.
**Lesson**: UX tests consistently reveal that what the team thinks is obvious, users find incomprehensible. "What I thought was great, nobody even noticed." Do the tests.

## Terminology

| Term | Definition | Context |
|------|-----------|---------|
| Deep interview (glubinnoe intervyu) | One-on-one conversation (in person or video) to study reality; the core qualitative research tool | NOT the same as journalism or HR interviews |
| Guide (gaid) | Structured document with logical question blocks; serves as a lighthouse during the interview, not a rigid script | Must be internalized as a structure, not memorized word-for-word |
| Qualifying block | Initial interview section with questions to identify which segment the respondent belongs to | Can take up to half the interview when exploring segments; collapses to 1-3 questions once segments are known |
| Navigation into jobs | Question that transitions from qualifying to the main block; always about PAST experience | Example: "Tell me the story of how you previously [solved this job]" |
| Rapport | Deep emotional contact where the respondent proactively shares emotions and truths | Goal of the first 3-10 minutes; critical for extracting honest emotional data |
| Edge effect | Cognitive bias where people remember only the first or last item in a sequence | Why you must ask questions one at a time |
| Social transaction | The implicit exchange in an interview where the respondent wants to "repay" your attention | Creates bias toward telling you what you want to hear; especially dangerous with future-oriented questions |
| Think Aloud Protocol | Asking the respondent to verbalize their thoughts continuously during a UX test | The backbone of UX testing methodology |
| RITE (Rapid Iterative Testing Evaluation) | Microsoft's approach: test 4 users → fix → test 4 new users → repeat until clean | Standard iteration cadence for UX tests |
| Medium (medium) | Any intermediary through which the customer interacts with your product | Can be digital (app, web) or physical (cafe, office, phone call, email) |
| Fiche request | When a respondent suggests specific features instead of describing their job/need | Redirect with: "When you wanted this in the past, why did that desire arise? What need did it serve?" |

## Key Processes (continued)

### Quantitative Validation of JTBD Research

**When**: After qualitative JTBD interviews are complete and you have a list of jobs and segments. You need to measure how widespread each job/segment is in the general population and compare them numerically.

**Critical Distinction**: Many qualitative interviews do NOT equal quantitative research. Even 100 qualitative interviews are still qualitative research. The difference is not quantity — it is the sampling approach.

| Qualitative Research | Quantitative Research |
|---------------------|---------------------|
| Answers: why, how, what motivations | Answers: how many, how often, how important, how satisfied |
| Flexible interview guide | Rigid, formalized questionnaire |
| Respondents selected sequentially, by convenience, snowball | Respondents selected through probabilistic sampling |
| Output: text, stories, job formulations | Output: numbers, matrices, statistical analysis |
| Cannot be extrapolated to general population | Can be extrapolated within known error margins |
| No measurement error concept | Every number has a calculable measurement error |

**Steps**:

1. **Compile the list of jobs from qualitative research**. Formulate each job clearly. This becomes your questionnaire content.
2. **Design a quantitative questionnaire**. Convert open-ended job descriptions into closed-ended questions: yes/no (does this job occur?), frequency scales (how often?), importance scales (1-10), satisfaction scales (1-10).
3. **Construct a probabilistic sample**. Use one of four approaches:
   - **Simple random sampling**: Every person in the target population has an equal chance of being selected. Best when you have a complete customer database to sample from.
   - **Cluster sampling**: Select entire groups (regions, companies, departments), then survey everyone within selected clusters. Used for large national studies.
   - **Systematic sampling**: Survey every Nth person (every 5th store visitor, every 3rd database entry). Provides randomness through fixed intervals.
   - **Stratified sampling**: Divide the population into categories (strata), then randomly sample within each stratum. Ensures representation of all subgroups.
4. **Determine sample size based on acceptable error margin**. Use the sampling error formula: `Error = Z * (sigma / sqrt(N))`, where Z is the confidence coefficient, sigma is standard deviation, and N is sample size.
5. **Collect responses through the formalized questionnaire**. Respondents interact with a programmed survey — not a live interviewer (unlike qualitative interviews).
6. **Calculate segment sizes with error margins**. Every percentage has a confidence interval. Report as "38% +/- 2.1 percentage points at 95% confidence."
7. **Compare numbers for statistical significance**. Use a significance calculator to determine whether two segment sizes are truly different or just noise. Two numbers that look different (e.g., 32% vs 31%) may be statistically identical given the sample size.

**Practical Error Margins by Sample Size** (at 95% confidence, worst case 50/50 split):

| Sample Size | Error Margin | Verdict |
|------------|-------------|---------|
| 50 | +/- 14% | Very rough. Only distinguishes large vs. small segments. |
| 100 | +/- 10% | Still large. Acceptable only for exploratory research. |
| 200 | +/- 7% | Moderate. Can distinguish segments that differ by 15+ percentage points. |
| 500 | +/- 4.4% | Good. Standard for most product research. |
| 1000 | +/- 3% | Very good. Can detect moderate differences between segments. |
| 2000 | +/- 2% | Excellent. Can detect small differences. Expensive. |

**Notes**:
- Error is MAXIMUM at 50/50 split. If you measured 5% for a segment, the error is smaller than if you measured 50%.
- Higher confidence level (99% vs 95%) increases the error margin for the same sample size. You can lower confidence to 90% or even 85% for exploratory research to reduce the required sample size.
- The error-reduction curve flattens dramatically after 500-800 interviews. Going from 50 to 100 interviews halves the error; going from 1000 to 2000 barely changes it.
- Two numbers that differ by less than their combined error margins are statistically indistinguishable. "32% vs 31%" at 200 interviews means you CANNOT claim one segment is larger than the other.

**Segment Scoring Formula**: Combine quantitative measurements into a single segment attractiveness score: `Score = Segment Size * Frequency * Importance * (1 - Satisfaction)`. Each parameter is measured independently in the questionnaire. Low satisfaction = high opportunity (unmet need). High satisfaction = the job is already well-served.

**Decision Criteria for Budget and Approach**:

| Situation | Approach |
|----------|---------|
| Have skills + time but no budget | Run the survey yourself using online panels or your own customer database |
| Have budget but no skills/time | Hire a research agency. Costs vary by sample size and complexity. |
| Need to determine required sample size | Use the online calculator. Start with acceptable error margin, work backward to required N. |

### Recruiting Respondents on International Markets

**When**: Conducting AJTBD research outside your home market. Recruiting respondents in unfamiliar countries with different cultures, payment norms, and communication tools.

**Steps**:

1. **Treat country as a qualifying segment factor**. Before recruiting, define: which country, which city (if relevant), which language. Country-level differences in behavior are often larger than demographic differences within a country. A 35-year-old parent in the US and a 35-year-old parent in Japan may have completely different jobs around the same product category.

2. **Choose a recruiting channel based on speed, cost, and access**:

| Channel | Speed | Cost per Respondent | Best For |
|---------|-------|-------------------|---------|
| **Respondent panels** (userinterviews.com, respondent.io, playbookux.com) | 1-2 days to first interview | $70-140 (incentive + platform fee) | Fast validation, B2C research, common demographics |
| **LinkedIn** (organic + Sales Navigator) | 1-3 months to first interview | Free (organic) to $100/mo (Sales Navigator) | B2B decision makers, niche professional roles |
| **Local recruiters** | 1-2 weeks | Varies widely | Hard-to-reach markets, specific cultural groups, offline populations |
| **iTalki hack** | Same day | $5-20 per session | Cheap B2C exploration, talking to real people from specific countries |
| **Referrals from existing respondents** | Immediate | Free | Snowball sampling after initial interviews |

3. **For respondent panels**: Register on the platform. Post your study with screening criteria. The platform matches you with pre-vetted respondents. Expect 1-3 day turnaround. Quality is generally high but verify with screening questions.

4. **For LinkedIn outreach** (B2B primary channel):
   - Start preparing 1-3 months BEFORE you need interviews. LinkedIn outreach requires a warm profile and connection history.
   - Build your profile as a researcher/product person, not a salesperson.
   - Use Sales Navigator to filter by company size, industry, role, location.
   - Send connection requests WITHOUT a pitch. Just connect.
   - After connection is accepted, send a research invitation (not a sales message).
   - Consider maintaining multiple LinkedIn accounts for different market segments (one per region/language).
   - Apply **social selling methodology**: provide value in your feed (share research insights, industry data) before asking for anything.

5. **For cold outreach letters** (email or LinkedIn message), follow this structure:
   - **One-liner personalization**: Reference something specific from their social media, company blog, or recent activity. "I saw your talk at [conference] about [topic]" or "I noticed [company] recently launched [feature]."
   - **Win-win value proposition**: Explain what they get from the conversation, not just what you get. "I'm researching [topic] and think your experience with [specific thing] would be invaluable. Happy to share our findings afterward."
   - **Clear CTA**: One specific ask. "Would you have 30 minutes next week for a conversation?" Not: "Let me know if you're interested and we can figure out a time."
   - Do NOT write long messages. 3-5 sentences maximum.

6. **Set payment norms by market and segment**:
   - US B2C: $30-50 per interview. Amazon Gift Cards are the standard payment method (easy to send, universally useful).
   - US B2B: $70-150 per interview. Amazon Gift Cards or direct payment.
   - Asian markets: Avoid cash offers in initial outreach — they look like a scam. Frame as "thank you gift" or "token of appreciation," not "payment for your time."
   - Senior executives (VP+ level): Money does NOT motivate. Instead, offer: access to research findings, intellectual interest ("I'd love your perspective on [problem]"), or network value ("I'm connecting people working on [topic]").

7. **Handle screening to catch fake respondents**. On international panels, some respondents lie about their qualifications to earn the incentive:
   - Add verification questions that only genuine respondents can answer (industry-specific terminology, recent events in their field).
   - Start the interview with qualifying questions BEFORE substantive questions. If they fail qualification, end politely and do not pay.
   - Cross-reference their LinkedIn profile with their screening answers.

8. **Build a recruitment factory**. Do not recruit ad-hoc for each study:
   - Maintain a spreadsheet of contacted people, their status, and their segment.
   - Set a weekly rhythm: N outreach messages per day, track response rates.
   - Treat recruiting as an ongoing pipeline, not a one-time activity.
   - Batch scheduling: book multiple interviews for the same day/week to maintain momentum.

**The iTalki Hack**: For cheap, fast B2C research in any country:
1. Go to iTalki (language learning platform). Find a tutor who is a native of your target country.
2. Book a lesson. Instead of learning the language, tell the tutor: "I'm researching [topic]. Can I ask you questions about your experience with [product/behavior]?"
3. Most tutors agree — they are being paid for their time regardless. Cost: $5-20 for 30-60 minutes.
4. Limitation: Tutors are a biased sample (educated, tech-savvy, flexible schedule). Use this for initial exploration only, not for segment validation.

**Budget Reduction Hacks**:
- Book 30-minute interviews instead of 60. At the 30-minute mark, ask: "We're at time, but this is really interesting. Would you mind if we continued for 15 more minutes?" Most respondents agree. You get 45 minutes for the price of 30.
- Ask every respondent for referrals. Free recruiting.
- Adapt to respondent tools. Not everyone uses Zoom, Google Calendar, or WhatsApp. In some countries, local tools dominate (WeChat in China, Line in Japan, KakaoTalk in South Korea). Do not force your tools — use theirs.

### Conducting Research on International Markets

**When**: You have recruited international respondents and are now conducting the actual AJTBD interview across cultural and language barriers.

**Steps**:

1. **Start with extended small talk** (5-10 minutes in Western cultures). Western respondents expect and value warm-up conversation before "getting to business." Find shared identity: "I see you're in [city] — I visited last year" or "I noticed you work in [industry] — I've been researching this space for a while." In Russian-speaking cultures, researchers often jump straight to questions after 1-2 minutes of pleasantries. In US/UK/EU contexts, this feels abrupt and cold.

2. **Calibrate your emotional baseline to Western norms**. Western baseline emotional expression is POSITIVE, not neutral. When a Western respondent says "it's fine" with a flat tone, they likely mean "it's bad." When they say "it's pretty good" with moderate enthusiasm, they likely mean "it's adequate."

| What They Say | What They Likely Mean |
|--------------|---------------------|
| "It's amazing!" (enthusiastic) | It's good |
| "It's good" (moderate) | It's adequate |
| "It's fine" (flat) | It's bad |
| "It's interesting" (thoughtful) | I have concerns |
| Silence / topic change | Strong negative reaction |

3. **Apply "The Mom Test" discounting for praise**. Western respondents are socialized to be positive and encouraging (the "being nice" culture). When they praise your product or concept, discount it heavily. Use Rob Fitzpatrick's "The Mom Test" techniques:
   - Do not ask "What do you think of this idea?" (they will say it's great).
   - Instead, ask about their PAST behavior: "Have you tried to solve this before? What happened?"
   - Ask for commitment, not opinion: "Would you sign up right now?" or "Can I add you to the beta list?"
   - If they praise without specifics, probe: "What specifically would make you use this?"

4. **Choose your interview persona: Guest vs. Insider**.
   - **Guest strategy** (first 1-3 interviews in a new market): Position yourself as a curious outsider. "I'm new to this market and trying to understand how things work here. Your perspective is incredibly valuable because I don't have the local context." This gives you permission to ask "obvious" questions without seeming incompetent.
   - **Insider strategy** (after initial interviews): Position yourself as someone with knowledge. "I've been talking to people in your industry for a few months. Many of them mention [pattern]. Does that match your experience?" This creates peer-level rapport and encourages deeper sharing.
   - Start every new market as a Guest. Switch to Insider after 5-10 interviews when you have enough context.

5. **Use gender-neutral language**. In Western contexts (especially US), gender-neutral language is expected and noticed when absent. Use "they/them" for generic references. Avoid assumptions about partners, family structure, or gender roles. This is not just politeness — gendered assumptions can shut down emotional sharing from respondents who don't fit traditional categories.

6. **Handle political sensitivity**. International respondents may bring up political topics (elections, international relations, social issues). Prepare a deflection phrase in advance: "That's a really interesting perspective. I'm not deeply informed about that topic, so I'd rather focus on your experience with [redirect to research topic]." Do not engage with political discussions — they hijack the interview and create emotional dynamics that distort research data.

7. **Navigate topic sensitivity across cultures**:
   - **Easier in Western cultures than Russian-speaking**: emotions, personal feelings, sex/relationships, mental health. Western respondents share these more readily.
   - **Harder in Western cultures than Russian-speaking**: medications/health conditions (privacy culture, HIPAA sensitivity), money/income (taboo in many Western contexts), personal financial decisions.
   - **Hard everywhere**: specific salary numbers, personal failures, illegal or semi-legal behavior.
   - Adapt your question sequence: start with easier topics for that culture, build to harder ones.

8. **English proficiency: B1-B2 is sufficient**. You do not need native-level English to conduct international interviews. Western respondents are accustomed to non-native speakers and will tolerate grammatical errors, accents, and slower speech. What matters is:
   - Clear pronunciation of key terms (practice industry-specific vocabulary)
   - Ability to ask follow-up questions spontaneously
   - Ability to catch emotional cues in the respondent's voice
   - If you don't understand something, say "Could you rephrase that?" — this is completely normal and not embarrassing.

9. **Comply with GDPR for EU respondents**. When interviewing EU residents:
   - Inform them that you're recording (if you are)
   - Explain what you'll do with the data
   - Offer to anonymize their responses
   - Provide a way to request data deletion
   - Keep consent records
   - This is a legal requirement, not optional politeness.

10. **Remember: you are "the boss" when paying for the interview**. You set the rules of the conversation. You paid for the respondent's time — you have full right to:
    - Redirect off-topic conversation
    - Ask uncomfortable questions
    - Push back on vague answers
    - End the interview early if the respondent doesn't fit
    - This mindset is especially important for researchers who feel like they're "imposing" on respondents. You are not imposing — you are running a professional exchange.

## Anti-patterns (continued)

### Running 100 Qualitative Interviews and Calling It Quantitative

**What**: Conducting many qualitative (deep, conversational) interviews and treating the aggregate as quantitative data. "We interviewed 100 people and 38% mentioned this job."
**Why wrong**: The 38% number is meaningless because the sample was not probabilistic. You selected respondents by convenience, referral, or targeted recruitment — not random sampling. The proportion reflects YOUR recruiting patterns, not the general population. You cannot calculate a meaningful error margin.
**Instead**: Acknowledge that qualitative research, regardless of volume, answers "what exists" (which jobs, which segments) — not "how much" (what percentage, what size). For "how much" questions, design a separate quantitative study with a probabilistic sample and a structured questionnaire.

### Assuming International Respondents Behave Like Domestic Ones

**What**: Using the same interview style, payment norms, small talk duration, and communication tools for all countries.
**Why wrong**: Cultural differences in emotional expression, payment expectations, and communication norms are large enough to invalidate your data. A US respondent saying "it's fine" means something very different from a Russian respondent saying "it's fine." Offering $50 cash via PayPal works in the US but looks like a scam in Japan.
**Instead**: Calibrate every aspect of your research process to the specific market: small talk duration, emotional baseline, payment method, communication tools, language register. Start as a Guest (see above) and adapt based on early interviews.

### Skipping Small Talk with Western Respondents

**What**: Jumping straight into interview questions after a 1-minute greeting. "Hi, thanks for your time, let's start with question one."
**Why wrong**: In Western cultures, this signals that you view the respondent as a data source, not a person. Gate 3 ("are you my tribe?") never opens. The respondent gives polite, surface-level answers for the entire interview.
**Instead**: Spend 5-10 minutes on genuine conversation before the first research question. Comment on something visible (their background on Zoom, their city, their company). Share something about yourself. Find a point of genuine connection. Only then transition: "I'd love to shift to the main topic..."
