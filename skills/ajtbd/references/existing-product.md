## Fundamental Difference: Existing Product vs. New Product

### What You Already Have
When developing an existing product, you already have a product, value, acquisition channels, and a working business model. It works somehow - maybe not ideally, maybe with suboptimal conversions, average check, margins, or retention - but it works.

### Implications of "Already Working"
1. **Fundamental risks are reduced**: You already validated that a segment exists, value is real, people buy. These are no longer existential threats
2. **Focus shifts**: From finding segment/value/first sales to scaling, efficiency growth, and strategic development
3. **Change is harder**: Politically and organizationally, modifying business model, segment, or value proposition is difficult because "it works - don't touch it"
4. **Client expectations constrain you**: Paying clients have expectations. You cannot just abandon them
5. **Current clients shape strategy**: The jobs they hire you for strongly influence your product roadmap. They request adjacent and next jobs
6. **Limited responsibility zones**: Product managers often have small zones of responsibility, making it hard to address issues outside their scope

### The Paradox
You CAN change segments, strategy, and business model. If you see it is not working, if you have evidence that a bigger segment exists or that you can create significantly more value, do it. But it requires:
- Greater personal career risk
- Stronger argumentation
- More organizational courage
- Acceptance that you might be told "Thanks, we don't need such advice"

## ABCD Segmentation Framework

### Origin and Core Concept
Created by Ilya Krasinsky (derived from RFM segmentation), refined through accelerator programs circa 2017-2018. Segments existing paying customers by marginality AND satisfaction through the lens of your specific product and business model.

### The Four Segments

#### A-Segment: Best Clients
- Maximally satisfied. Job is important, actual, large budget for the job
- Maximum marginality. Buy simply, buy happily, use the product
- Ideal clients in every way

#### B-Segment: Good Clients
- Satisfied but not perfectly. Something is missing, some jobs not performed or not performed ideally
- Still profitable, but less marginal than A
- Sales cycle slightly longer (B2B), slightly more objections
- Still attractive and worth serving

#### Line Between B and C (Critical Boundary)

#### C-Segment: Bad Clients
- Low-margin or loss-making
- Product does not fit them. They bought by mistake
- They complain, cry, buy slowly, torture your sales team and account managers
- Working with them is pain

#### D-Segment: Worst Clients
- Aggressively destroy your team's brains AND do not buy
- Pure cost center with zero return

### The 80/20 Discovery
Teams that create products spend 80% of their time satisfying and working with C and D clients, even though C/D bring only 20% of revenue. A/B bring 80% of revenue but receive only 20% of team attention.

**Why this happens**: C and D clients scream loudly. A and B are generally satisfied, buy quickly, do not make noise. C and D: "This doesn't fit!", "Too expensive!", "This doesn't work!", "You don't have this report!" They are active, they capture your attention, and you react.

### Critical Properties of ABCD Segmentation

1. **Unique per product**: No two products have the same ABCD. It is a function of YOUR product with YOUR business model
2. **Changes over time**: As your product, segments, and business model evolve, ABCD shifts. Update periodically (roughly annually)
3. **Not segmentation by jobs**: ABCD is segmentation by marginality and satisfaction, as an ADDITIONAL layer on top of job-based segmentation. Within each of A, B, C, D you will have job-based segments
4. **Find CAUSES, not consequences**: "A-clients are those who pay a lot and are satisfied" is restating the definition. The cause is: WHY are they satisfied? WHY are they marginal? Example: Clinic with inpatient ward - A-segment: people with income over 1M rubles/month, savings over 10M, aged 40+, who consciously invest in health. They pay for the ward, buy all additional services, don't rush to discharge. THAT is the cause

### How to Build ABCD Segmentation

1. **Extract data**: Pull most marginal clients from CRM, but use a fixed time window (e.g., first year of client life). Without a fixed window, you just get oldest clients
2. **Find most marginal + most satisfied** = A/B
3. **Find low-margin/loss-making + least satisfied** = C/D
4. **Identify distinguishing attributes**: Look in CRM data, sales team knowledge, account manager insights
5. **If CRM lacks data**: Conduct ~20 in-depth interviews with A, B, and some C/D clients. From qualifying questions, learn: who they are, why they have these jobs, why they are satisfied/dissatisfied
6. **Extract job-based segments within A/B**: Through interviews, identify 2-3 distinct job-based segments within A/B

### Strategy from ABCD Segmentation

1. Complete ABCD segmentation
2. Identify job-based segments and qualifying factors within A, B, C, D
3. Focus product development and acquisition on A/B segments
4. Configure lead qualification so every incoming client is assessed as likely A/B or likely C/D
5. Tell all C/D clients: "Au revoir. Our product is not for you"
6. Scale by finding NEW clients outside your current base who RESEMBLE your A/B segments

### The C/D Objection: "But That's Revenue!"
No matter what you do, C/D will NEVER become satisfied or marginal for your current product. By definition. Just as Sberbank cannot make pensioners an A-segment for their current product.

**But**: You CAN create a DIFFERENT product with a DIFFERENT business model for which current C/D become A/B. ABCD segmentation is product-specific. Change the product - change who is A/B.

## Case Studies for ABCD Segmentation

### Kidster (Mobile App for Children's Activities)
- App helped mothers find children's classes (singing, swimming, dance)
- Business model: First class free, commission on subsequent paid classes
- Support received 113 complaints: "Not enough options in my area", "Filter doesn't work", "Contact info outdated"
- **Trap**: Team almost started fixing these problems
- **Discovery**: Almost none of the 113 complainers were paying customers. The product was perfect for freebie-seekers who loved browsing free first classes
- **Lesson**: Before fixing problems, check if complainers are A/B or C/D. 113 screaming C/D clients can misdirect your entire team

### Worky.ru (Blue-Collar Job Marketplace)
- Helped hire blue-collar workers (drivers, couriers, warehouse workers, 20-80K rubles salary range)
- Business model: Commission per worker placement
- **Initial hypothesis**: Students are A-segment (active, willing to work for less)
- **Actual A-segment**: "Tough guys" (profiles with Jason Statham photos, wolf-themed VK groups)
- **Why**: Students invest in their development, want to earn more over time, and will leave the 20-80K salary range. Tough guys stay at their salary level indefinitely = recurring revenue from repeat placements
- **Method**: Extracted workers who most frequently found jobs through the app, checked their VK profiles, found the pattern

### Laminate Cutting Company
- Had Italian machine with special cutting technology
- Sold to: design studios, construction crews, individuals
- High revenue but poor profit
- **A/B segment**: Specific design studios familiar with the special cutting technology - low re-cutting rates, regular return customers
- **C-segment**: Individuals - expensive to acquire, don't return, low check, high re-cutting rates (costly errors)
- **Action**: Refocused resources from individuals to design studios similar to existing A/B clients
- **Result**: Business became profitable

### Dental Clinic ArtDent
- Identified A/B segment: people wanting comprehensive jaw reconstruction
- Could not break through 40M rubles revenue ceiling
- After ABCD-based focus: grew conversion by 40%, revenue grew ~40%

## Sean Ellis Segmentation

### Method
Ask existing product users: "How disappointed would you be if this product stopped existing?"
- **Very disappointed** (the key metric)
- Somewhat disappointed
- Not disappointed
- N/A (don't use it)

### The 40% Threshold
Sean Ellis found that products with objective product-market fit have >40% of users answering "very disappointed."

### Superhuman Case (Rahul Vohra)
1. In closed beta, surveyed thousands of beta testers
2. Only 22% "very disappointed"
3. Analyzed which roles appeared most among the "very disappointed": founders, executives, top management
4. Re-ran survey only on those roles: 22% rose to 32%
5. Went to "somewhat disappointed" users whose expected value criterion was speed, studied their jobs and current workflows
6. Generated value hypotheses from those insights
7. Iterated until "very disappointed" reached 58%
8. Launched publicly, grew well

### ABCD vs. Sean Ellis: Author's Assessment
ABCD segmentation wins because it incorporates not just subjective factor (how disappointed) but also objective factor (marginality). A client can be "very disappointed" but bring negative margin. ABCD catches this.

## Local vs. Global Optimum Framework

### The Metaphor
You stand in flat terrain and see a hill ahead. You climb it - that is your local optimum. From the top, you might see a mountain range far away with much higher peaks - that is the global optimum.

### Local Optimum
- Do NOT fundamentally change segment, core jobs, or business model
- Examples: Optimize marketing within current segments, add adjacent segments on same market, improve conversions, reduce churn, A/B test pricing, improve onboarding
- **Risk**: Low
- **ROI cap**: Limited by definition (you are optimizing within existing constraints)
- **Who does this**: Less experienced employees, narrow responsibility zones
- Instruments: ABCD segmentation, Sean Ellis segmentation, working with current paying clients

### Global Optimum
- FUNDAMENTALLY change segment, core jobs, and/or business model
- Examples: Enter new country, target entirely new segments outside current clients, exit to higher-level job, change business model (e.g., from hardware sales to hardware + subscription like Peloton)
- **Risk**: High
- **ROI potential**: Much larger (new country = 10x+ market, new business model = new margin structure)
- **Who does this**: Top management, most competent employees
- Instruments: Job graph exploration, strategic mechanics, new segment research

### Both Simultaneously
This is NOT either/or. You can press both pedals at the same time:
- Part of the team works on local optimum (current A/B, efficiency)
- Part of the team searches for global optimum (new segments, new countries, new models)
- Resource allocation between the two is the key decision

### Delegation Principle
- **More competent employees** get higher-risk, higher-reward tasks (global optimum, new product launches, critical partnerships)
- **Less competent employees** get lower-risk, bounded tasks (card detail conversion, specific funnel optimization)
- Risk ranking formula (from RAT): Prioritize = (Probability x Consequence) / Verification Cost

## Innovator's Dilemma Through JTBD Lens

### Clayton Christensen's Core Insight
Well-run companies get disrupted NOT because of incompetence but because of competence:
- They focus intensely on current customers
- They focus on current customer needs, willingness to pay, margins
- A new thing emerges from below, looks like a toy
- Because company is well-run, they say: "Our customers aren't interested in that"
- The gap opens from competence, not incompetence

### Marc Andreessen's Observation
- Good startups have roughly a 5-year window before they get locked into a pattern of doing business with particular customers
- Once locked, disruption opportunities emerge
- Other entrepreneurs immediately attack those opportunities

### JTBD as the Exit from Innovator's Dilemma
- The dilemma is excessive focus on executing specific jobs with specific solutions for specific customers using specific channels
- This cycle optimizes continuously (make Word better and better and better)
- But the team that makes Word cannot easily make ChatGPT
- **Exit**: Study the job graph. Move to previous jobs, next jobs, adjacent frequent jobs, higher-level jobs. These moves ARE the strategic response to the innovator's dilemma

## Growth Mechanics for Existing Products

### Mechanic 1: Exit to Next Job
Study the critical sequence. What job comes AFTER the one you currently perform?
- Example: Form tool helps create forms and collect submissions. Next job: process responses, decide who to invite/hire/contact. This job was done in Google Sheets. Form tool builds the response-processing solution internally
- Example: Airtable's core job is working with information. Previous job: collecting information. They added form collection

### Mechanic 2: Exit to Previous Job
What job comes BEFORE yours in the critical sequence?
- Example: If you are a developer building houses, previous job: save money for down payment, get a mortgage
- Example: Tinkoff Investments helps invest. Previous segment/stage: "I am just learning about investing" - create educational onboarding

### Mechanic 3: Shift to Adjacent Segment
Add a new segment or expand to adjacent ones:
- Segment by budget for job execution (Yandex Taxi: economy -> comfort -> business)
- Segment by different criteria of expected result
- Segment by different context or big job
- Segment by geography (Russia -> Brazil -> Mexico)
- Example: B2B WhatsApp automation tool: started with Russia SaaS, added enterprise, then went to Brazil/Mexico
- Example: DomKlik footer shows dozens of different segments for Sberbank mortgages

### Mechanic 4: Super-App
Segment not by big job but by "person pays us for something." Look at what OTHER big jobs/core jobs (unrelated to your original job) you can perform:
- Example: Yandex Taxi moves people A-to-B. Person is already in the app. What else? Feed them, deliver groceries, deliver packages, intercity moves
- Criterion for choosing new jobs: A/B segments, jobs not too far in context from original

## Three Research Templates for Existing Products

### Template 1: Packaging and Conversion Growth
**When to use**: Product has paying clients but you don't know segments, haven't done ABCD, don't know value, need to communicate value better.

**Process**:
1. ABCD segmentation
2. Find current A/B clients for interviews
3. Conduct 2 in-depth interviews per client (~1 hour each):
   - Core jobs + big jobs they hire your solution for (focus on core jobs)
   - Barriers (if they remember)
   - Direct value question: "What value/benefit do you get from our product for these jobs?"
   - Projective question: "Sell me this product right now, as if I'm your friend you want to convince"
   - Aha-moment: "Do you remember when you first understood the value of our product?"
   - Critical sequence from big job appearance to hiring your solution
   - If solution changed: switch interview
4. **Output**: Segments by jobs within A/B. Barriers, value, aha-moment. Critical sequence before purchase
5. **Use output for**: Rewrite landing pages, ads, commercial proposals, sales scripts. Generate conversion growth hypotheses
6. Generate acquisition and communication hypotheses
7. With new clients resembling A/B: conduct solution interviews + UX tests, observe funnel progression
8. 20-30 solution interviews to reduce errors in new hypotheses

### Template 2: Value Growth
**When to use**: Need to increase the value delivered to existing clients.

**Process**:
1. ABCD segmentation
2. 15 interviews (B2B) or 30+ interviews (mass B2C, potentially 50+ for mass products like taxi, real estate, medicine)
3. Study: Core jobs, big jobs, AND previous/next small jobs + job graph below core jobs
4. **Output**: Full job graph at core job level and below - raw material for value hypothesis generation
5. Generate hypotheses, run UX tests, solution interviews with improved product

### Template 3: Churn Reduction and Retention Growth
**When to use**: Need to reduce churn and increase return rates.

**Process**:
1. If frequent-use product: study which frequent small jobs adjacent to core job users hire NON-your-solution for
2. Study micro-jobs within core jobs: how can the critical sequence be executed sub-optimally? What causes drop-off?
3. **Critical step**: Switch interviews with churned A/B clients
   - Was A/B, was paying, left for a competitor
   - Learn: What happened? What trigger? What problem caused the switch? What value did they go to?
4. **Output**: Reasons for churn, triggers, competitor advantages, opportunities to prevent churn

## Terminology

| Term | Definition | Context |
|------|-----------|---------|
| ABCD Segmentation | Segmentation of existing paying clients by marginality and satisfaction through the lens of specific product and business model | Created by Ilya Krasinsky; primary tool for existing product development |
| Local Optimum | Low-risk improvements within current segment/jobs/business model that have limited ROI ceiling | Delegated to less experienced employees |
| Global Optimum | High-risk fundamental changes to segment/jobs/business model with much larger ROI potential | Requires top management competence and courage |
| Innovator's Dilemma | Well-run companies get disrupted because their competence in serving current customers blinds them to emerging threats from below | JTBD job graph exploration is the prescribed exit |
| Sean Ellis Test | Survey asking "How disappointed would you be if this product stopped existing?" with 40% "very disappointed" as PMF threshold | Loses to ABCD because it lacks marginality dimension |
| Super-App Mechanic | Growth strategy of adding unrelated jobs for existing paying users, segmenting by "person who pays us" rather than by job | Examples: Yandex Taxi adding food delivery, groceries |
| Qualifying Factors | Attributes that distinguish A/B from C/D clients (income, company size, job characteristics) - the CAUSES of being A/B, not the consequences | Must be discoverable pre-purchase for lead qualification |
| Re-cutting (in laminate case) | When client makes measurement errors requiring costly rework - a proxy for whether a segment fits the product | Illustrates how business model details determine ABCD boundaries |
