## B2C Frequency Job Interview

### Example: Samokat Grocery Delivery — Studying a Frequency Job

**Context**: Interviewer Vika Pankevich interviews Lena (24, product manager, Moscow) about grocery delivery. The goal is to navigate into the respondent's jobs, identify the most important frequency job, and study it deeply.

**Technique Demonstrated: Navigation Into Jobs**

1. **Set boundaries without revealing product** (B2C rule). Say "I want to learn about how you handle grocery shopping and delivery" — never name the product (Samokat) or the hypothesis.
2. **Ask qualifying questions** to confirm the respondent fits: "How do you get your groceries? How often? What services do you use?" Identify that Lena uses Samokat, Yandex.Lavka, and offline stores.
3. **Navigate into jobs** by asking: "Tell me the story of your most recent order from [service]. What prompted it?" Collect 3-5 distinct jobs the respondent hires the service for.
4. **Choose which job to study deeply**. Prioritize by: (a) frequency — which job occurs most often, (b) importance — which matters most to the respondent, (c) problems — which causes the most pain. Ask the respondent directly: "Of the situations you described, which happens most often? Which matters most to you?"

**Technique Demonstrated: Full Job Study for Frequency Jobs**

Once a single job is selected (e.g., "I need groceries delivered fast when I realize I'm missing something for tonight's dinner"):

1. **Expected result**: "When you order in this situation, what result do you expect? What does 'good' look like?"
2. **Context and trigger**: "What was happening right before you decided to order? What triggered the decision?"
3. **Emotions before action**: "How were you feeling when you realized you were missing the ingredient?"
4. **Importance scale**: "On a scale of 1-10, where 10 is life and death, how important is solving this quickly?" Challenge inflated ratings: "You said 8 — are you sure? Where 10 is life-threatening?"
5. **Satisfaction scale**: "How satisfied are you with how [service] handles this? 1-10, where 10 is perfection."
6. **Value question**: "What was valuable about using [service] here specifically?"
7. **Aha moment**: "Was there a moment when you thought 'wow, this is exactly what I needed'? What happened?"
8. **Price-value match**: "Do you feel the price matches the value you get?"
9. **Problems**: "What was hard or frustrating about this experience?" Apply the "What is hard about..." drilling technique.
10. **Drivers**: "What would make you order MORE from this service?"
11. **Barriers**: "What doubts or fears do you have about using it?"
12. **Alternatives**: "When you can't or don't want to use this service, what do you do instead?"

**Technique Demonstrated: Studying Previous Job Versions**

After the main job study, explore how the respondent solved this job BEFORE discovering the current solution:

1. Ask: "Before you started using [Samokat], how did you handle the same situation — needing groceries fast?"
2. Map the evolution: respondent went to the store themselves -> tried Utkonos delivery -> switched to Samokat.
3. For each previous version, ask: "What was wrong with that approach? Why did you switch?"
4. This reveals the job's history and what improvements the respondent values most.

**Technique Demonstrated: Lower-Level Jobs**

Identify micro-jobs within the main job:

1. Ask: "Walk me through the steps when you order. From the moment you decide to the moment you receive it."
2. Each step is a potential lower-level job: finding the item, checking availability, choosing substitutes, tracking delivery, receiving the order, checking quality.
3. For each lower-level job, ask: "Is this easy or hard? What could go wrong here?"

**Technique Demonstrated: Post-Interview Analysis**

After the interview, immediately (within 30 minutes):

1. **Formulate jobs in standard format**: "When [context/trigger], I want to [action], so that [expected result]."
2. **Build the job graph** on a FigJam/Miro board: place the high-level job at the top, lower-level jobs below, previous jobs to the left, alternative solutions at the bottom.
3. **Apply value creation mechanics** to each job: (a) Can we kill a lower-level job entirely? (b) Can we fix problems within existing jobs? (c) Can we take over the job from the user? (d) Can we ensure base quality where it currently fails?
4. Each value creation mechanic becomes a potential product feature or improvement hypothesis.

**Lesson**: Frequency jobs require studying the job cycle — trigger, action, result — and then studying how the user's solution evolved over time. The job graph for frequency jobs tends to be wide (many lower-level jobs happening in parallel within one order) rather than deep.

## B2C Sequential Job Interview

### Example: Apartment Rental — Studying Jobs in Sequence

**Context**: Interviewer Vika Pankevich interviews Lena about renting an apartment using HomeBro concierge service. The goal is to study sequential jobs — the jobs that come BEFORE and AFTER the main job in the user's journey.

**Technique Demonstrated: Studying a Set of Solutions**

In B2C, the respondent often uses multiple products/services within one job journey. Do not focus on a single product:

1. Ask: "Tell me how you found and rented your current apartment. Walk me through the whole journey."
2. Map ALL solutions the respondent used: Cian (listing platform) -> HomeBro (concierge) -> real estate agent -> landlord.
3. For each solution, ask: "What job did you hire this for? What did it do that others couldn't?"
4. Build a solution map showing which part of the journey each product covers.

**Technique Demonstrated: Previous Jobs WITH Job Graph Connection**

**Critical error shown and corrected in this interview**: When studying previous jobs, you MUST connect them to the higher-level job in the job graph. Without this connection, you get answers from the wrong job graph entirely.

**Wrong approach** (demonstrated as mistake): "What did you do before using HomeBro?" -- This question is ambiguous. The respondent may answer about previous apartment searches, previous concierge services, or previous uses of any of the solutions.

**Correct approach**: "You said you were looking for an apartment because [higher-level job — e.g., 'you moved cities for a new job']. Before you started looking for apartments THIS time, what else did you consider or try? Did you think about alternatives to renting?" This anchors the previous-job question to the HIGHER-LEVEL job ("settling into a new city"), not the immediate job ("finding an apartment").

**Steps for correct previous-job study**:

1. First, identify the higher-level job: "Why were you looking for an apartment at all? What life situation triggered this?"
2. Then ask about previous approaches to the HIGHER-level job: "When you were figuring out [higher-level job], what did you consider? What other options besides renting did you think about?"
3. Only then ask about previous approaches to the immediate job: "Before this apartment search specifically, how did you search for apartments in the past?"
4. This two-layer approach reveals both strategic alternatives (the respondent considered buying, co-living, staying with relatives) and tactical alternatives (used Cian directly, asked friends, used a different concierge).

**Technique Demonstrated: Next Jobs After the Main Job**

After studying how the respondent found and rented the apartment, explore what happened AFTER:

1. Ask: "Once you signed the lease and got the keys, what was the next challenge? What did you need to do?"
2. Map the next jobs: furnishing, connecting internet, registering at the address, getting to know the neighborhood, setting up utilities.
3. For each next job, ask: "How did you handle this? Was it easy or hard? What solutions did you use?"
4. Next jobs are product expansion opportunities — HomeBro could potentially help with post-rental setup, not just apartment search.

**Technique Demonstrated: Lower-Level Jobs Within the Main Job**

Break the main job into constituent micro-jobs:

1. "Walk me through finding this specific apartment. Step by step."
2. Map: defining criteria -> searching listings -> filtering -> scheduling viewings -> visiting apartments -> comparing options -> negotiating price -> signing contract -> receiving keys.
3. For each step, identify: Was this step a source of value (positive emotion) or a source of pain (negative emotion)?
4. Qualify: "Which of these steps was the hardest? Which took the most time? Which worried you the most?"

**Lesson**: Sequential job interviews require rigorous anchoring to the job graph hierarchy. The most common error is asking about "previous jobs" without specifying which level of the job graph you are asking about. Always go UP to the higher-level job first, then ask about previous approaches to THAT job, then come back down to the immediate job's previous approaches.

## B2B Specifics

### Framework: B2B Deal Dynamics

B2B interviews require understanding deal dynamics that do not exist in B2C. The key differences:

**Personal Emotional Jobs Dominate B2B Decisions**

1. In B2B, personal emotional jobs drive approximately 90% of purchase decisions when the decision-maker is far from the company's founder.
2. When the founder/LD personally participates in deals, the split is closer to 50/50 between business KPIs and personal motivations.
3. **River metaphor**: Business KPIs (revenue, efficiency, cost reduction) are the river — they exist and flow regardless. Personal goals (career growth, status, job security) are the boat — they determine WHERE the person navigates on the river.
4. Google's 2009 B2B research found that buyers are 8x more likely to pay a premium for a product when they can see a personal benefit in using it.

**Five Core Personal Motivations in B2B**:

1. **Job security**: "If I choose this vendor and it works, my position is safe. If it fails, I could lose my job."
2. **Status**: "I work with the best tools/vendors in the market. My peers respect my choices."
3. **Career growth**: "This project, if successful, will get me promoted or noticed by leadership."
4. **Eliminating boring routine**: "This product automates the parts of my job I hate doing."
5. **Supporting safety**: "This product reduces the chance that something blows up on my watch."

Interview these motivations by asking: "When you were evaluating vendors for [solution], what was on your mind personally? Not the company's goals — your own goals at that time?"

### Framework: Roles in B2B Deals

Map the following roles for every B2B deal you study:

| Role | Definition | Interview Priority |
|------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Business Customer** | Person or team whose job the product solves | High — understand their job first |
| **Decision Maker (DM)** | Person who says "yes, we buy" | High — understand their motivations and criteria |
| **Budget Holder** | Person who allocates the money | Medium — understand budget source and constraints |
| **Influencers (LVR)** | People whose opinion the DM trusts | Medium — understand what they recommend and why |
| **Recommenders** | External advisors (consultants, community, media) | Medium — identify who the DM listens to outside the company |
| **Saboteurs** | People with hidden interests who block the deal | High — critical to identify early |
| **End Users** | People who actually use the product daily | Low (unless expensive to hire/retain) |

**Identifying Saboteurs**: Ask the DM or business customer: "Was there anyone in the organization who was NOT enthusiastic about this change? Who pushed back? Why?" Saboteurs are often the person who implemented the previous system — their status and competence are threatened by a replacement. They may raise "concerns" (security, compliance, timing) that are actually motivated by self-preservation.

**When End Users Matter**: End users only become a priority when they are expensive to hire or retain. If your product makes life harder for a developer who costs $200K/year and has 50 competing job offers, the end user's satisfaction becomes a deal-breaker. For line employees with no alternatives, end user opinion matters less in the purchase decision (though it matters for adoption).

### Process: B2B Interview Priorities

When interview time is limited, prioritize B2B interview blocks in this order:

1. **Target job** — What business problem does the customer hire the product to solve? This is the same as B2C.
2. **Problems** — What is hard about the current approach? What fails? Same as B2C.
3. **Roles and motivations** — Who is involved in the deal? What are their personal motivations? B2B-specific.
4. **Solution block** — How did they choose, evaluate, and implement the solution? B2B-specific.
5. **Budget** — How is the budget determined? From which budget line? Who approves? B2B-specific.

### Anti-pattern: Avoiding Money Questions in B2B

**What**: Researchers skip budget questions because they feel awkward asking about money.
**Why wrong**: Budget information is critical for pricing strategy, segment prioritization, and understanding deal dynamics. The respondent is NOT embarrassed — you are. B2B respondents routinely discuss budgets with vendors.
**Instead**: Ask directly. "How much budget did you allocate for this? How was that number determined? From which budget did the money come — was it a separate line item or part of a larger budget?" Normalize: "I ask because understanding budget helps us build the right product at the right price."

### Process: Recruiting B2B Decision Makers

Standard respondent panels do not work for B2B DMs. Use these channels:

1. **Account-Based Marketing approach**: Identify specific companies that fit your target segment. Find the DM within each company (LinkedIn, company website). Send a personalized outreach.
2. **Conferences**: Attend industry events where DMs gather. These are the best "sales days" for researchers — DMs are already in networking mode and expect conversations about their business.
3. **Referrals from existing respondents**: After a successful B2B interview, ask: "Do you know other [role] at other companies who deal with [this problem]?" B2B respondents have strong peer networks.
4. **LinkedIn Sales Navigator**: Filter by company size, industry, role, seniority. Send connection requests with a research-oriented message (not sales pitch).

## B2B Segment Search Interview

### Example: Respondent Recruiting Platform — B2B Segment Search Interview

**Context**: Interviewer Vika Pankevich interviews Vadim, CEO of a 15-person research agency, about recruiting respondents for research projects. The product being explored is a platform that automates respondent recruitment.

**Technique Demonstrated: B2B Opening — Reveal Your Product**

Unlike B2C where you hide the product, in B2B interviews you reveal it upfront:

1. "I'm from [company name]. We're building a platform for [brief description]. I want to understand how you currently handle [the job]."
2. **Why reveal**: B2B respondents expect transparency about who they're talking to. Concealing your identity feels deceptive in a business context. They need to calibrate their answers to be useful to you. Many B2B respondents REFUSE interviews with anonymized companies because of NDA concerns.

**Technique Demonstrated: Studying Respondent Priorities First**

Before diving into the target job, understand the respondent's business context:

1. "What are your main goals for this year?"
2. "What takes up most of your time? Can you break down your work week by percentage?" (Vadim: 40% client management, 30% team management, 20% sales, 10% admin.)
3. "Where does [target job — respondent recruitment] sit in your priorities?"
4. This reveals whether your target job is a core priority (=strong signal) or a peripheral concern (=weaker signal but still valid).

**Technique Demonstrated: Deal Chain Study**

Map the entire chain from first contact to payment:

1. "When a client needs respondent recruiting, who initiates the conversation?" (Vadim: The client's researcher or product manager contacts us.)
2. "Who approves the project?" (Vadim: Their head of research or product director.)
3. "Who holds the budget?" (Vadim: The head of department. Sometimes it comes from the overall research budget, sometimes from a project-specific budget.)
4. "How is the budget number determined?" (Vadim: We quote based on respondent type and quantity. Most clients have a rough budget in mind from past projects.)
5. "Who else is involved — accountants, legal, procurement?" (Vadim: For large companies, procurement wants an invoice and contract. For startups, the PM just pays via bank transfer.)

**Technique Demonstrated: Recommender Identification**

Identify who influences the DM's vendor choice:

1. "How did you choose your current tools for respondent recruitment?" (Vadim: We tried several — mostly through recommendations from other agency owners.)
2. "Where do you discuss work tools with peers?" (Vadim: Entrepreneur chats on Telegram, UX research communities, sometimes conferences.)
3. "Is there anyone specific whose opinion you trust on this topic?" (Vadim names specific community leaders and conference speakers.)
4. Recommenders are your marketing leverage in B2B — reach them, and they reach your target segment.

**Technique Demonstrated: Hypothesis Testing for Sub-Jobs**

When the respondent does not mention a sub-job you hypothesized, test it explicitly:

1. The respondent described the recruiting process but did not mention "respondents failing to show up to the interview" as a problem.
2. Interviewer probes: "Tell me about no-shows. Does it happen that recruited respondents don't come to the interview?"
3. Vadim: "Yes, 15-20% no-show rate. It's infuriating because the researcher's time is wasted and the client gets upset."
4. Follow up with the standard job study: "What do you do about it? How do you handle no-shows?"
5. This reveals a sub-job ("ensuring recruited respondents actually show up") that the respondent internalized as "just how things are" and did not mention spontaneously.

**Technique Demonstrated: Personal Emotions in B2B**

Even in a B2B segment-search interview, capture personal emotions:

1. Vadim, when describing no-shows: "I was angry. Not at the respondent — at myself. Because I should have screened better."
2. When describing a failed recruiting campaign: "I felt embarrassed presenting the results to the client. Like I'm not professional enough."
3. These emotional signals indicate jobs with high personal importance — they are stronger product opportunities than jobs described purely in business terms.

**Lesson**: B2B segment search interviews follow the same core structure as B2C (qualify -> navigate -> study job -> study problems -> study alternatives) but add three B2B-specific blocks: (1) deal chain mapping, (2) recommender identification, (3) budget structure study. Always ask about personal emotions — they are the strongest signal for job importance in B2B.

## Internal Products

### Framework: AJTBD for Internal Products

JTBD methodology applies fully to internal products (internal tools, platforms, services built for employees). The key calibrations:

**Step 1: Classify the Internal Product's Purpose**

Identify which of three motivations drives investment in this internal product:

| Motivation | Example | Research Focus |
|-----------|---------|---------------|
| Optimize expensive employees | CRM for senior sales team ($150K+ each) | Value creation: how does the tool make each person more productive? |
| Optimize mass employees | Ticketing system for 500 support agents | Scale efficiency: how does the tool reduce cost per unit of work? |
| Optimize key services | Internal API platform for product teams | Service quality: how does the tool reduce downtime, errors, integration time? |

**Step 2: Segment Employees Using ABCD Framework**

Not all employees are equally valuable to study. Apply ABCD segmentation:

| Segment | Description | Research Investment |
|---------|-----------|-------------------|
| A (top performers) | Generate disproportionate value. Expensive to replace. | High — their time is most valuable to optimize |
| B (solid performers) | Reliable contributors. Moderate replacement cost. | Medium — study for scalable improvements |
| C (adequate performers) | Meet minimum requirements. Low replacement cost. | Low — study only for mass-efficiency improvements |
| D (underperformers) | Below minimum. Replacement may improve output. | None — do not design the product for this group |

Focus AJTBD interviews on A and B segments. Do not let C and D segment feedback drive product decisions — optimizing for underperformers degrades the tool for top performers.

**Step 3: Determine Value Currency**

The critical question for internal products: **Is usage mandated or voluntary?**

| Scenario | Value Currency | Success Metric |
|----------|---------------|---------------|
| **Voluntary adoption** (employee can choose alternatives) | Same as external market: the employee "hires" the tool for a job. Competitors exist (spreadsheets, external tools, manual processes). | Adoption rate, satisfaction, NPS. Treat the employee as a customer. |
| **Mandated usage** (policy requires using this tool) | Base quality: the tool must not make the employee's life worse than the previous approach. | Zero-suffering metric: employees do not actively complain or work around the tool. |

For voluntary adoption: run full AJTBD research as if building an external product. The employee IS your customer — they will switch to alternatives if your tool fails their jobs.

For mandated usage: run AJTBD research focused on identifying pain points and base quality failures. The goal is not delight — it is zero suffering. If employees actively hate the tool and build workarounds, the mandate has failed even if technically enforced.

**Step 4: Choose Interview Type Based on Respondent Role**

| Respondent | Interview Type | Why |
|-----------|---------------|-----|
| **DM** (VP of Engineering who commissioned the tool) | B2B style | They have business goals, budget constraints, career motivations. Same dynamics as any B2B buyer. |
| **Expensive end users** (senior engineers, data scientists) | B2B with personal focus | They have alternatives (external tools, scripts, open-source). Personal productivity and status matter. Ask about personal motivations alongside job study. |
| **Line employees** (support agents, data entry clerks) | B2C style | They have limited alternatives and limited agency. Focus on the job itself — what is hard, what takes time, what causes errors. Personal motivations are less differentiated. |

**Anti-pattern: Treating All Internal Users Identically**

**What**: Running the same interview script for the VP who commissioned the tool and the junior support agent who uses it daily.
**Why wrong**: The VP's job is "ensure my team is productive and I look competent to leadership." The support agent's job is "get through my ticket queue without errors." These are fundamentally different jobs requiring different interview approaches.
**Instead**: Identify which role each respondent holds (DM, expensive end user, line employee) and select the corresponding interview type. A single internal product serves multiple jobs for multiple roles — map them separately.

**Anti-pattern: Ignoring the Mandate Dynamic**

**What**: Running NPS surveys and satisfaction research on a mandated tool as if employees chose to use it.
**Why wrong**: Employees who are forced to use a tool cannot "switch" — so satisfaction metrics are meaningless in the traditional sense. A mandated tool with 30% NPS is not a product failure if the alternative (no tool) was worse. But a mandated tool with active workarounds IS a failure.
**Instead**: For mandated tools, measure workaround frequency instead of satisfaction. Ask: "Do you ever use something BESIDES [this tool] to accomplish this task? What? Why?" High workaround frequency = the tool fails the job despite the mandate.

## Terminology

| Term | Definition | Context |
|------|-----------|---------|
| Navigation into jobs | Interview technique to identify 3-5 jobs the respondent hires a product for, then selecting the most important/frequent/problematic one for deep study | Used at the start of the main interview block |
| Job graph | Visual map of jobs at different hierarchy levels: high-level (strategic), main (target), lower-level (micro-steps), previous (before current solution), next (after current solution) | Built during post-interview analysis on FigJam/Miro |
| Value creation mechanics | Four ways to create product value within a job graph: kill a lower-level job, fix problems in existing jobs, take over the job from the user, ensure base quality | Applied during post-interview analysis to generate product hypotheses |
| Deal chain | B2B-specific sequence: who initiates -> who evaluates -> who approves -> who pays -> who uses | Mapped during B2B interviews to understand sales and adoption dynamics |
| Saboteur | Person in a B2B deal with hidden interests who blocks the purchase (often the person who built/chose the previous system) | Identified through indirect questions about resistance and pushback |
| ABCD segmentation | Classification of employees by value contribution (A=top, B=solid, C=adequate, D=underperformer) for prioritizing internal product research | Used to decide which employees to interview for internal product development |
| Value currency | What determines whether an internal product is "good" — for voluntary tools, it is user satisfaction; for mandated tools, it is absence of suffering | Key decision that determines the entire research approach for internal products |
| Workaround frequency | Measure of how often users bypass a mandated tool using alternative methods | Replaces NPS/satisfaction as the primary quality metric for mandated internal tools |
