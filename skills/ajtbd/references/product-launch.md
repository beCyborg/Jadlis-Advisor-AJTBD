## PMF Gradient Framework

PMF is not binary. It is a gradient with four stages, each having distinct tasks, risks, and required competencies.

### Stage 1: PMF Absent
- You do not know if there are people for whom your solution is valuable
- No demand, no sales or only isolated ones
- You do not know: whether clients exist for whom this is valuable, what acquisition costs will be, what operational costs will be, how customers will return, what conversions will look like, what marginality will be
- **Primary task**: Find segment, create value, prove to yourself (not investors) that you have at least an approximation of segment and value knowledge
- **Why prove to yourself**: You are the main investor - you invest your energy. If you do not believe in PMF yourself, you will not have the energy to push through risks, pain, and problems
- **Best instrument**: Direct sales where the founder personally sells; solution interviews
- **Death risk**: No segment exists, no one needs the product
- **Probability of death at this stage**: Very high. You are moving from "absent" to "weak" and the most likely outcome is that you will not find a segment

### Stage 2: PMF Weak
- Sales appeared but are rare, not scalable, inconsistent
- Selling is not easy but some transactions happen
- **Primary tasks**: Continue creating value (if value were strong, customers would already be pulling product off shelves). Business model appears: unit economics, channel testing, conversion optimization
- **Key focus areas**: Unit economics convergence, value growth, demand generation, conversion growth
- **Death risk**: No acquisition channel found, or channel found but economics don't work, or channel works then stops working. Customers don't retain, don't convert to repeat purchase. Service costs too high

### Stage 3: PMF Strong
- Good conversions, scalable sales, sales team wins deals on average
- Demand is generally good, value is proven
- **Primary tasks**: Efficiency growth (operational costs, conversions, retention, return rates, CAC). Scale to adjacent segments
- **Death risk**: Something breaks - segment exhausted, channels exhausted, external dependency fails (e.g., bank stops providing installment plans), competitor finds better strategy

### Stage 4: Off the Shelves (Torn from Hands)
- Main task is not to die from demand
- Cannot keep up with customer volume
- Demand is so high you have empty shelves; you think "How do I serve all these customers?"
- Customers find you and ask how to buy without you doing marketing
- Examples: ChatGPT at launch (registration queues, "we cannot handle demand, please wait a week"), Zoom during pandemic (10x usage growth), Color Genomics received "love letters" from customers thanking them for saving their lives
- **Elad Gil's signal**: You know you have very strong PMF when you get strong customer feedback even from a small group of people. Customers write you unsolicited gratitude

## Riskiest Assumption Test (RAT) Framework

### Core Principle
When you create any new product, its market success always depends on a set of hypotheses - risky assumptions. If all confirm, you have a working business. Each carries a probability of not confirming.

### Process: How to Apply RAT

1. **Map the minefield**: List all risky assumptions your product depends on
2. **Rank by three criteria**:
   - Probability of risk materializing (higher = check first)
   - Cost of consequences if risk materializes (higher = check first)
   - Cost to verify (cheaper = check first)
3. **Formula**: Prioritize = (Probability x Consequence Cost) / Verification Cost
4. **Go verify**: Start with the cheapest, most probable, most expensive-in-consequences risk
5. **Iterate**: As assumptions are confirmed or refuted, update the map and re-rank

### Five High-Level Risky Assumptions

These are not exhaustive. Add your custom assumptions and make them granular with specific details.

#### 1. Market has enough money for our ambitions
- Is the market growing or shrinking? Strong market carries weak founders; weak market kills strong founders
- Market volume: is the total addressable market worth competing for?
- Regulation risks (e.g., crypto regulation uncertainty)
- **Trap**: Spending years only to discover your market ceiling is a few hundred million rubles

#### 2. We found an economically attractive segment
- Market may exist but segment may not
- Segment may exist but you may not know about it
- Segment may exist and be known but you may lack resources/competencies/technology to create significantly more valuable solution for it
- If you cannot create significantly more valuable solution, why compete?

#### 3. We created value and the segment buys our product
- Segment exists but you made a bad product nobody buys
- Common failures: bad UX (example: Bitfree requiring 1 hour to understand), wrong developers, no good designer, no good PM
- You may not even know your product is bad

#### 4. We achieve target margin per paying client
- Business model mismatch: lead cost too high, service cost too high, unsatisfactory conversions (trial-to-paid, month 1 to month 2, year 1 to year 2)
- Example: Peloton sells hardware near cost, profits on $30-40/month subscription. If customers churn at 6 months or 1 year, business model collapses
- Example: GoExpert - value existed, customers bought and retained, but lead acquisition was so expensive the business could not sustain itself

#### 5. We can create and scale demand
- Do we have competencies to create demand?
- Do channels with needed economics exist?
- Do channels have sufficient capacity?
- Will we hit a ceiling where the channel volume is too small?

### RAT Fundamentally Changes Your Mindset
The typical founder/manager thinks success is just a couple of steps away: money and team. RAT says: you will die with very high probability. So let us map the minefield NOW and start defusing mines before stepping on them.

### RAT Applies Beyond Startups
RAT is not only for new products from scratch. The same set of risky assumptions applies to:
- A small feature within an existing product
- Scaling a product to an adjacent segment
- Improving conversion rates
- Any business task where you rely on assumptions for success

### RAT and Specific Details
When working with risky assumptions, never use abstract terms. Always specify:
- Concrete country/market (not "international market" but "B2B SaaS in Brazil")
- Concrete segment description (not "small businesses" but "restaurants with 2-5 locations that currently use spreadsheets for inventory")
- Concrete value proposition (not "better solution" but "platform with pre-built automations that reduces reporting time from 4 hours to 15 minutes")
- Concrete business model (not "subscription" but "monthly subscription at $99/location with freemium onboarding")
- Concrete acquisition channels (not "digital marketing" but "Google Ads targeting 'restaurant inventory management' keywords + partnerships with restaurant supply distributors")

## Pivot: Definition and Mechanics

### What a Pivot Is
A pivot is a change in the set of risky assumptions - partial or nearly complete.

### Types of Pivots (by what changes)
- **Market change**: Same product, different country (Russia to USA)
- **Segment change**: Same market, different target users
- **Value change**: Different product for same segment
- **Business model change**: Same value, different monetization
- **Full pivot**: Only team/resources preserved, everything else changes

### Pivot Mechanics in Practice
1. Start with a set of risky assumptions (market, segment, value, business model, channels) with specific details
2. Go to market, discover which assumptions fail
3. Change the failing assumptions while preserving what works
4. Repeat until either: (a) find a working product, or (b) give up

### Case Study: Dima Zaryuta's 80 Pivots
Team composition: Dima (product, design, marketing), Zhenya Kurshev (technology), Slava (sales). Three co-founders with complementary skills - the one constant across all pivots.

- **Pivot 1**: Widget for companies to use idle pandemic employees for customer support (Russia). Pilot with Askona: out of 50 idle employees, the entire job could be done by just 1 employee. Product worked but value proposition collapsed
- **Pivot 2**: Same product, USA market. Changed risky assumption: country. Discovered US companies fired employees during pandemic (unlike Russian companies that kept them). The job-to-be-done literally did not exist in this market
- **Pivot 3**: Productivity plugins for Google Calendar, Gmail - set of convenience tools. Changed everything except team. Discovered there is no money in this market
- **Pivot 4**: AI-personalized outreach using newly available neural network APIs. Good initial conversions (2-4x standard manual outreach). But two fatal problems: (a) every competitor started doing the same, killing the advantage, (b) automated client onboarding proved impossible, turning what was meant to be SaaS into an agency model with agency margins
- Key lesson: Each pivot changed a subset of risky assumptions while preserving team, resources, competencies. What was preserved = what was validated. What changed = what failed

### Case Study: Crypto Industry Pivots
The blockchain/crypto industry demonstrates RAT and pivoting visibly: a great technology exists (blockchain), and for years the industry has been trying to find who needs it. "Maybe here, maybe here, maybe here." Products with objective product-market fit in crypto remain relatively rare. Each new protocol, each L1/L2 blockchain launch is essentially a search for a set of risky assumptions that actually works.

### Case Study: Refocus
Refocus (edtech startup by Roma Khomar) had decent product metrics. But their entire business model depended on a bank providing installment plans to students. When the bank stopped providing installments, unit economics collapsed. The risky assumption "we can maintain this financing arrangement" was never explicitly identified and tested.

### Case Study: GoExpert
Nikita Abramenko (founded Potok, sold to Alfa Bank) launched GoExpert. Shut down after 2 years. Reason: "Too long a sales cycle, very different types of experts, could not algorithmize the pre-sales process." Translation: the cost of acquiring a paying lead was prohibitively high. Value existed, customers bought and retained, but acquisition cost killed the business. The risky assumption about demand creation cost was the mine that exploded.

## Anti-patterns

### Anti-pattern: Goal is to Launch
- **What**: Treating product launch as the goal
- **Why wrong**: 90% of product concepts are dead. You just don't know why yet. A ticking bomb that will most likely explode
- **Instead**: Your goal is to KILL the product as fast as possible. This psychological reframing is fundamental

### Anti-pattern: Dragging the Dead Horse
- **What**: Continuing to invest in a product that should have been killed long ago
- **Why wrong**: Sunk cost fallacy. Inside companies this is even worse because there is no venture capital discipline
- **Instead**: Recognize that the most likely outcome is death. Act accordingly. Kill fast

### Anti-pattern: Inside Companies Lack Venture Discipline
- **What**: Internal products lack the harsh feedback loop that fundraising provides
- **Why wrong**: Startups raising investment get constant "no" with explanations, forcing hypothesis revision. Inside companies: politics, strategy, budget games allow dead horses to be dragged for years
- **Instead**: Artificially create the discipline. Regular harsh evaluation of risky assumptions. Honest assessment of whether the product should continue

### Anti-pattern: Abstract Risky Assumptions
- **What**: Writing vague assumptions like "market is growing" or "segment exists"
- **Why wrong**: Cannot verify abstract statements. Cannot act on them
- **Instead**: Always specify: which market, which country, which segment with which jobs, which specific value proposition, which concrete channels

### Anti-pattern: Confusing Weak PMF with No PMF
- **What**: Having a few rare, non-scalable sales and treating this as validation to invest heavily
- **Why wrong**: At weak PMF, the probability of failure is still very high. Channels may not exist, retention may fail, economics may never converge
- **Instead**: Recognize which gradient stage you are at. Weak PMF means continue proving value AND simultaneously test business model viability. Do not scale prematurely

### Anti-pattern: Ignoring Business Model Risk When Value Exists
- **What**: Celebrating that customers love the product while ignoring that unit economics do not converge
- **Why wrong**: GoExpert case - value existed, customers bought and retained, business still died because acquisition was too expensive. Peloton case - product beloved, but if retention drops below threshold, model collapses
- **Instead**: RAT forces you to consider all five risky assumptions simultaneously. Value is necessary but not sufficient. Check margin, check scalability of demand creation

## Process: Full Product Launch Algorithm

The complete algorithm for launching a new product, integrating RAT with the PMF gradient:

### Step 1: Choose Your Market
- Evaluate market size, growth trajectory, regulation
- Apply the "game table" criteria: high stakes, weak players, fast feedback, game you enjoy
- Verify risky assumption #1 (market has enough money)

### Step 2: Find and Select Segment
- Identify segments by jobs within the market
- Evaluate economic attractiveness (size, budget, frequency) and ability to create significantly more valuable solution
- Apply four forces analysis per segment-solution triple
- Verify risky assumption #2 (economically attractive segment exists)
- This is the entire subject of the next workshop (segment-selection.md)

### Step 3: Create and Validate Value
- Build minimum viable solution for the chosen segment
- Founder sells directly (best validation method at absent/weak PMF)
- Verify risky assumption #3 (we can create value that the segment actually buys)
- If value is insufficient: either increase value or pivot to different segment

### Step 4: Prove Business Model
- Test unit economics: acquisition cost, service cost, conversions, retention
- Verify risky assumption #4 (target margin per paying client)
- If economics do not converge: change business model or pivot

### Step 5: Create and Scale Demand
- Test acquisition channels with needed economics and capacity
- Verify risky assumption #5 (we can create and scale demand)
- If no channel works: evaluate whether business is viable or needs pivoting

### At Every Step
- The goal is to KILL the product, not to launch it
- Each failed verification is information, not failure
- A pivot is a change in the set of risky assumptions based on what you learned
- The fastest, cheapest path to knowledge is the best path

## Key Decision Criteria

### When to Do Research vs. When to Skip
- Research is a tool, not always the right one, not always the best one
- Do research when verification cost is low relative to the risk (e.g., $50K research vs. $500K product launch)
- Skip research when you can verify faster by direct sales or experiments
- Remember: 9 out of 10, or 19 out of 20, hypotheses do not confirm. This is normal, not a sign of incompetence

### Choosing Your Game Table
When selecting which set of risky assumptions to pursue, prefer:
- **High stakes**: Large potential market
- **Weak players**: Competitors are not strong
- **Fast feedback**: You can learn quickly whether assumptions are correct
- **Game you enjoy**: Sustainable energy for the long haul

### Heuristic: 90% Death Rate
- 90% of startups die. Primary cause: product nobody needs
- Even expert product managers confirm only 2-3.5 out of 10 hypotheses (not 5 out of 10)
- Venture investors mentally write off each investment immediately (90-95% probability of loss)
- Spray-and-pray model: distribute small checks across many startups, pray someone survives
- Implication: If you know this, act smarter. Verify fastest, cheapest, most consequential risks first

## Terminology

| Term | Definition | Context |
|------|-----------|---------|
| PMF Gradient | Product-market fit as a spectrum from absent to off-the-shelves, not a binary state | Replaces binary PMF thinking |
| RAT (Riskiest Assumption Test) | Framework for mapping, ranking, and systematically verifying the risky assumptions a product depends on | Applied before and parallel to JTBD |
| Risky Assumption | A hypothesis that must be true for the business to work, carrying risk of being false | Core unit of RAT analysis |
| Pivot | Change in the set of risky assumptions (partial or complete) | When current assumptions prove wrong |
| Dead Horse | A product that should have been killed but continues receiving investment | Anti-pattern in corporate product development |
| Spray-and-Pray | VC model of distributing small investments across many startups hoping one survives | Reflects the 90% death rate reality |
