## Core Principle: Segment Selection is THE Strategic Decision

Segment selection is the single most important strategic decision in product creation. Correct or incorrect segment choice heavily determines all subsequent investments. Wrong segment selection can be a death sentence.

### Goal of Segment Selection
Invest limited resources (time, attention, money, team effort) to achieve maximum ROI while paying minimum opportunity cost compared to choosing an alternative segment.

### What Segment Selection Requires
- Segment must include a job (always). Demographics, income, device, location - all fine as additional criteria, but job must be present
- You cannot build a product for everyone. Spreading limited resources across all segments creates tiny value for everyone instead of significant value for someone
- By refusing other segments, you concentrate resources to create significantly more value for one segment
- Do not fear refusing segments. You can always add segments later through scaling

## Four Forces of Progress: A Segment Selection Tool

### What Four Forces Actually Are
Four forces of progress is NOT a tool for improving conversions, fighting habits, or optimizing UX. It is a tool for CHOOSING SEGMENTS.

### The Four Forces
When a person considers switching from a current solution to a new one, four forces act simultaneously:

1. **Additional Value (pulls toward new solution)**: What the person understands the new solution offers beyond their current one. Example: Tesla vs Volkswagen Tiguan - better acceleration, 2-4x cheaper fuel, quieter, super tablet, status/fashion

2. **Problems with Current Solution (pushes away from current)**: Pain points with the existing solution. Example: seeing exhaust fumes, buggy navigation, car getting old. NOTE: You cannot create problems with the current solution (unethical, usually illegal). You can only actualize/help the person recognize existing problems

3. **Barriers to New Solution (holds back from switching)**: Fears, anxieties, doubts that the new solution will not optimally perform the job. Example for Tesla: unclear how to charge, no charger in parking garage, need to restructure habits, won't work for long trips, might stop getting software updates in Russia. Barriers are anti-value - they eat the value you created

4. **Habit (holds to current solution)**: The brain has physically restructured itself to perform the job more energy-efficiently with the current solution. See detailed section on habit below

### Critical Insight: Forces Are Unique Per Triple
All four forces are unique for each combination of:
- **Segment** (who)
- **Current solution** (what they use now)
- **New solution** (what you offer)

Example: Person wearing Whoop fitness tracker looking at Oura Ring has different four forces than the same person looking at Samsung Galaxy Ring. Different additional value, different barriers, different everything.

### Critical Insight: Forces Are Dynamic
Four forces do not exist permanently. They arise at the specific moment when a person with a job, who currently uses some solution, looks at/studies a new solution. When the person is satisfied with their current solution and not considering alternatives, four forces do not exist.

### How Four Forces Guide Segment Selection
Given that forces are unique per triple, use them as selection criteria. At equal conditions, prefer segments where:
- **Stronger problems** with current solution (bigger push away)
- **More additional value** you can create (bigger pull toward)
- **Weaker habit** to current solution (less resistance)
- **Lower barriers** to your solution (less anti-value), and barriers you CAN manage/reduce

If a segment has strong habit, small problems, and you cannot create much additional value - abandon that segment. Find a different one where the balance of forces works in your favor.

## Habit: Neuroscience and Practical Implications

### What Habit Actually Is (Neurobiologically)
- Habit means the brain has PHYSICALLY restructured itself: strengthened some neural connections, weakened others
- Purpose: to perform a frequent job more energy-efficiently than before restructuring
- Brain consumes up to 1/4 of all body resources. If it can reduce consumption by automating a task, it will
- No single universal process for habit formation has been identified (different habits form through slightly different neural pathways)

### Why Habit Is So Powerful
- Once the brain has completed restructuring, it resists changing again
- When you ask someone to change, a subconscious refusal reaction appears BEFORE conscious awareness, manifesting as anxiety/fear that things will get worse
- This is amplified by loss aversion cognitive bias - the brain dramatically overestimates potential loss
- Result: People fundamentally resist changing how they do things, even when the new way is objectively better

### Critical Anti-pattern: Fighting Habits
- **What**: Trying to make users adopt fundamentally new behaviors
- **Why wrong**: You are fighting against physical brain restructuring. The force is immense. Even when you win, expect massive metric drops and extreme user backlash
- **Evidence**: Yandex Images did 3 major redesigns in 3 years. Two grew metrics, one failed. Every redesign caused severe metric drops. Support received thousands of messages including death threats. Because you are telling users' brains: "Forget everything. Learn to navigate your apartment in a completely different way"
- **Instead**: Find segments where habit is weaker, OR embed into existing habits

### Practical Rules for Working with Habit

#### Rule 1: Do not create new habits if possible
Find segments where the habit to current solution is not strong. This gives better conversions than trying to break strong habits.

#### Rule 2: Embed into existing habits
Case study - PitchMe (ATS/CRM for hiring):
- Market had a dominant competitor considered the de facto standard (like Salesforce for sales)
- Interface was bad, candidate database was outdated, but users were habituated
- PitchMe's approach: copied the competitor's interface layout, added convenience improvements, loaded a more current candidate database
- Value proposition: "Like your old slippers, but slightly more comfortable and with a more current candidate database"
- Result: Achieved scalable sales by NOT requiring users to learn new workflows

#### Rule 3: Make changes incrementally
If modifying an existing product, make small, element-by-element changes. Do NOT redesign everything at once:
- Users cannot identify what broke
- You cannot identify what caused metric drops
- Massive batch changes trigger maximum resistance

### How to Detect Habit in Research
Look for situations where a person acts without thinking, without searching, without considering alternatives.
Example: When a woman fails to get pregnant after 3 negative tests, she goes to her gynecologist without thinking, without Googling, without considering alternatives. "I just go to my gynecologist." This automatic, non-deliberative action signals strong habit.

## Barriers: Anti-Value Framework

### What Barriers Are
Barriers are fears, anxieties, doubts that a new solution will not optimally perform the job. They are "anti-value" - they consume the value you created. You build a great product, but barriers eat the perceived value before the customer acts.

### Barriers Can Be Real or Imagined
- **Real**: Based on past negative experience with the solution type
- **Imagined**: Person invented the fear without evidence. Often much more common

### Case Study: Yandex Browser vs Google Chrome
- Key barrier to switching from Chrome to Yandex Browser: "If I install a new browser, I will lose all my passwords and bookmarks. I don't remember all those passwords"
- Reality: ALL browsers can import all passwords from any other browser in a few clicks
- But people did not know this, and the fear prevented them from even considering the switch
- Team identified and addressed this barrier, contributing to eventually overtaking Chrome's market share in Russia

### How to Discover Barriers
- In interviews about past product consideration: "Was there anything that stopped you from starting to use [solution]? Any anxiety that the solution wouldn't optimally solve your problem?"
- In solution interviews (asking someone to buy): "Was there anything that stopped you?" - this extracts barriers specific to your solution
- People may not remember (barriers often operate below conscious awareness)

### How to Reduce Barriers
Barriers, unlike habits, CAN be actively managed and reduced:
- Communicate that the feared problem does not exist (Yandex Browser: "We import all your passwords in 2 clicks")
- Provide guarantees and safety nets (Samokat delivery: "If you ever get expired product, we'll gift you [something]")
- Show social proof, certifications, track record
- Offer trials, freemium, money-back guarantees

## Segment Selection Criteria: The Complete Algorithm

### Factor 1: Economic Attractiveness (through your business model lens)

Universal metrics to evaluate for each potential segment:

#### Segment Size
How many people have this job? Determines scaling potential and theoretical market share ceiling.

#### Budget for Job Execution
- How much has the person spent in the past on performing this job?
- What solutions did they consider? At what price points?
- What solutions did they reject (and at what price)? -> upper/lower budget boundaries
- What the person actually paid is the strongest budget signal
- Direct budget questions can work but many people (especially very low and very high income) answer inaccurately

#### Frequency
For products that perform recurring jobs: how often does the person perform this job?

#### Business Model-Specific Factors
Depends on your specific model (subscription, transaction, commission, etc.):
- **Subscription**: How long has person been engaged in this activity? (longer = likely better retention). What do they currently pay for subscriptions in adjacent areas?
- **Transaction**: Average check, repeat purchase frequency
- **Commission/marketplace**: Volume of transactions, number of participants
- **B2B SaaS**: Company size, number of licenses, industry

#### How to Gather This Information
- Qualifying questions in interviews
- Expert interviews
- Quantitative validation surveys
- CRM data analysis
- Secondary research (industry reports, competitor data)

### Factor 2: Ability to Create Significantly More Valuable Solution

Evaluate using the four forces framework:
- **Problems with current solution**: Bigger problems = bigger opportunity
- **Additional value you can create**: Including fixing current problems AND adding new value (cheaper, faster, better, more jobs in one solution, killing lower-level jobs, executing next job in sequence)
- **Habit strength**: Weaker habit = easier switching
- **Barriers**: Lower barriers, or barriers you can manage = better

### The 10x Rule (Peter Thiel)
Find a job where you can create a product 10x better than the current solution. When value difference is that large, people forgive imperfections, tolerate inconvenience, overcome habit resistance, and switch despite barriers.

## Bundling / Unbundling / Rebundling Cycle

### The Pattern
Products constantly oscillate between bundles (super-apps) and standalone solutions:
1. **Bundle era**: One product does everything (e.g., bank branch handles all financial jobs)
2. **Unbundling**: Technology innovation enables a standalone product that does ONE job significantly better, pulling users away from the bundle (e.g., Tinkoff credit card vs. Sberbank branch)
3. **Rebundling**: Each standalone product grows into a new super-app (e.g., Tinkoff becomes T-Bank super-app for everything)
4. Cycle repeats

### Why This Matters for Segment Selection
- The force that drives unbundling is almost always a technological innovation that enables one job to be performed SIGNIFICANTLY better
- When selecting segments, look for situations where a technology shift enables you to rip one job from an existing bundle and perform it so much better that it overcomes the gravitational pull of habit to the old solution
- The value delta must be large enough to make people switch for just ONE job, forgiving that your product does not do the other 10-20 jobs the old bundle handled

### Case Study: Banking Unbundling
- 20 years ago: Bank branch was the super-app (deposits, credits, cards, business accounts)
- Unbundling: Tinkoff (credit cards), Tochka (business banking) - each ripped jobs from the old bundle using mobile technology
- Rebundling: T-Bank, Tochka, Sberbank all became super-apps again
- What enabled the rip: mobile apps provided so much better experience for specific jobs that users switched despite losing the convenience of one-stop-shop

## Common Errors in Segment Search

### Error: Idea First, Research Immediately
- **What**: Having a product idea and immediately jumping into user interviews
- **Why wrong**: You likely have no job hypothesis, no segment hypothesis, and no way to evaluate if people even pay money for this type of job
- **Instead**: First evaluate the big job. How many people currently pay money for performing this big job? If nobody pays, high probability of building a dead business

### Error: "I Will Create the Market"
- **What**: Product addresses a job nobody currently pays for, entrepreneur believes demand will emerge
- **Why wrong**: "Create the market" almost always means: market does not exist, you will make a product, market will not appear
- **Instead**: Always validate that people already spend money on the big job, even if they use very different solutions for it

### Error: People Ask and Pay, But Sales Are Dropping
- **What**: Current customers keep paying but market share grows while revenue drops
- **Why wrong**: This is the Innovator's Dilemma. Competitors are moving to higher-level jobs in the job graph while you are stuck optimizing for current customers
- **Instead**: Study the job graph. Identify how competitors are moving to previous, next, or higher-level jobs. Plan your own moves before disruption happens

### Error: "I Know Everything About My Market"
- **What**: Assuming expertise means complete knowledge of all segments, jobs, triggers, criteria, consideration sets
- **Why wrong**: Reality is almost always different from assumptions. Author spent 20M rubles and 3 years on Focus Calendar, convinced the value was sufficient. It was not
- **Instead**: Treat your knowledge as hypotheses. Verify cheaply before investing heavily. A 300K ruble research in 3 months beats a 20M ruble discovery in 3 years

## Switch Interviews: Methodology for Segment Discovery

### What Switch Interviews Study
How people change from an old solution to a new one for performing a job. Three zones:

1. **Decision to switch**: What trigger/problem launched thinking about finding a new solution?
2. **Search for new solution**: How did they search? What did they Google? What solutions did they compare? What barriers did they encounter?
3. **Purchase and switching act**: What ultimately drove the decision? How did the actual switch happen?

### Why Switch Interviews Help Segment Selection
- Reveal the four forces in action for specific triples (segment + current solution + new solution)
- Show which triggers and problems are strong enough to overcome habit
- Reveal barriers that prevented switching (which you can then address)
- Provide concrete data for comparing segments: which segments have stronger push/pull dynamics

### Example: Accounting Services for Small Business
- Trigger: Annual reporting deadline approaches, business owner asks accountant about status, accountant forgot or made errors
- Problem: Accountant forgets or makes mistakes in reporting
- Search: Person doesn't start immediately. Starts looking 2 months before next deadline
- Questions to ask: "How did you search? What did you Google? What solutions did you compare? What barriers did you see? What made you decide?"

## Terminology

| Term | Definition | Context |
|------|-----------|---------|
| Four Forces of Progress | Four factors (additional value, problems with current solution, barriers, habit) that determine whether a segment switches from old to new solution | Tool for segment SELECTION, not conversion optimization |
| Triple | The unique combination of segment + current solution + new solution for which four forces are evaluated | Each triple has unique four forces |
| Barrier | Fear, anxiety, or doubt that a new solution will not optimally perform the job; anti-value that consumes perceived product value | Can be real or imagined; can be actively managed |
| Habit (neurobiological) | Physical brain restructuring that enables more energy-efficient execution of a frequent job | Cannot be easily fought; find segments with weaker habits instead |
| Switch Interview | Research method studying how people change from old solution to new, covering trigger, search, and purchase phases | Reveals four forces in action for specific triples |
| Bundling/Unbundling | Cyclical oscillation between super-apps (bundles) and standalone solutions, driven by technology innovations enabling 10x better execution of single jobs | Framework for identifying segment opportunities |
| Opportunity Cost | The value you forgo by choosing one segment over another | Core concept in segment selection - minimize this |
| Big Job Budget | Amount people currently spend on performing the big job, regardless of which specific solution they use | Primary indicator of market size for a segment |
