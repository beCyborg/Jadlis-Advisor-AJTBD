## Core Definition: What Product Strategy Is

**Product strategy is a way to compete more effectively given specific competitive conditions, starting position, available resources, and competencies.**

Product strategy is NOT:
- Goals ("become market leader" is a goal, not a strategy)
- Beautiful words ("make the simplest, most convenient product" translates to "we don't know what to do")
- What executives decide at offsites (strategy is what the company actually does, not what it declares)
- A secret art accessible only to the elite (it is a learnable, non-intuitive but knowable algorithm)

Product strategy IS:
- **What we do and what we refuse to do** — strategy is as much about saying "no" as saying "yes"
- **The actual allocation of resources** — if all resources go to fixing low-margin client bugs, that IS your strategy, regardless of the slide deck
- **A subset of business strategy** — business sets the target (e.g., "2x revenue next year"), product strategy determines how to achieve it through product decisions
- **A continuous practice** — not a one-time event, done at every level, at every moment

### When Companies Seek Strategy
Usually when something is wrong: growth stalled, revenue declining, competitors overtaking, trend is unsatisfying. The Toyota principle (Eiji Toyoda): "Our current success is the main reason to change. We must be constantly dissatisfied to succeed." Strategy thinking should be continuous, not crisis-driven.

## The Single Core Decision of Product Strategy

**For which job will we compete, and why are we convinced we will win the competition for this job?**

Everything in product strategy reduces to this. You can ship features, fix bugs, build technology — but the strategic question is always: what job, for what segment, with what value proposition, and why will we win?

## Prerequisites: Information Needed for Strategy

Before making strategic decisions, gather data through the OODA cycle (Observe, Orient, Decide, Act):

### 1. Qualitative Research
- Which segments are ours? Which are competitors'?
- What does the job graph look like for each segment?
- What are the expected result criteria?
- What is the consideration set? Where do we stand in it?

### 2. Desk Research (Analytics)
- Is the market growing or shrinking?
- What trends, new technologies, regulatory changes exist?
- How do users react to new products and technologies?
- Country-level factors (e.g., crypto regulation shifts after political changes)

### 3. Expert Interviews
- What do domain experts know about segments, products, unused opportunities?
- What talent are competitors hiring? (signals their strategic direction)

### 4. Competitive Analysis
- Direct competitors: who competes for the same job?
- Indirect competitors: who competes for the same job with a different solution type?
- Non-obvious competitors: who is killing the job entirely? (cloud services killing hosting panel market)

### The Cost of Strategic Errors
Strategic decisions are among the most expensive decisions by cost of making AND cost of consequences. Example: Yandex Browser's project to overtake Chrome in Russia cost billions of rubles, involved 80+ interviews, multiple quantitative studies over 1.5 years. The research cost tens of millions of rubles — but wrong strategic decisions at that scale would cost orders of magnitude more.

## Strategy Mechanics: The Complete Taxonomy

### Principle: Value Mechanics ARE Strategy Mechanics
Every value creation mechanic (fix problems, kill a job, perform more jobs with one solution, etc.) becomes a strategy mechanic when applied at a higher level of the job graph. The difference is scale and scope:

- Fixing a small UX problem = tactical value improvement
- Fixing a fundamental problem at the big-job level = strategic market entry
- Killing a micro-job = feature improvement
- Killing all sub-jobs by moving to a higher-level job = market disruption

**Rule**: The higher the level of the job in the graph where you apply the mechanic, the more strategic the impact.

---

### Strategy Mechanic 1: Increase Switching Costs (Retention Mechanic)
**What**: Make it expensive (in time, effort, money, or lost data) to leave your product.
**When to use**: When you have a customer base and want to protect against competitor poaching.
**How it works**: Create dependencies, accumulated value, or procedural barriers to leaving.

**Examples**:
- Telegram: chats, files, communities, channels, notes — all locked in; switching to WhatsApp means losing everything
- B2B vendor lock-in: SAP migration is so painful that Severstal (after SAP left Russia) faced a crisis — all factories, production chains, suppliers tied to SAP
- Apple: iCloud photos (try downloading everything), Lightning cables (before USB-C mandate), Apple ID ecosystem
- Adobe Creative Suite: canceling subscription requires paying for all unused months
- ISPs: sign up remotely, but must bring the router to the office in person to cancel
- Banks: open account online easily, close it by visiting a branch

**Warning**: This is a gray mechanic. It extracts value from customers, not creates it. Consider reputation and long-term trust costs.

### Strategy Mechanic 2: Offer Value Only at the Moment of Churn
**What**: Give discounts, upgrades, or special offers only when the customer tries to leave.
**When to use**: As a retention backstop (but not as primary value strategy).

**Examples**:
- Yandex Plus, mobile operators: "Wait, here's 50% off for 3 months"
- Audible: multiple screens of "are you sure?" with escalating offers before allowing cancellation
- Tinkoff: "We'll service your credit card for 1 ruble/month"

**Warning**: This is a dark pattern. It means you could have provided this value all along but chose not to. Creates resentment. Use cautiously.

### Strategy Mechanic 3: Redirect Attention ("You came for X, we show you Y")
**What**: Use the attention you've captured for one job to expose the client to other products/services.
**Examples**:
- News sites: came for the article, 80% of screen is ads
- Blizzard launcher: came to play Starcraft, tiny button in corner, rest is ads for other games
- Movie theaters: came for the film, 40 minutes of trailers first
- Turkish shops: came to walk the street, pulled into a fur coat store

### Strategy Mechanic 4: Lock Unique Value (Content/Asset Exclusivity)
**What**: Secure exclusive content, assets, or capabilities that cannot be replicated.
**When to use**: When unique content/capability is available and you can negotiate exclusivity.
**Examples**:
- Netflix, HBO: exclusive original series
- Spotify + Joe Rogan: exclusive podcast deal (later abandoned as strategy — users found it inconvenient)
- Wink + "Slovo Patsana" (Russian series): exclusive premiere

**Note**: Only works when the content is truly irreplaceable. Piracy and convenience friction often undermine this.

### Strategy Mechanic 5: Go to the Previous Job (Upstream Entry)
**What**: Enter the market by serving the job that happens BEFORE your competitors' core job.
**When to use**: To escape direct competition AND create an acquisition channel simultaneously.

**Procedure**:
1. Map the critical sequence of jobs for your target segment's big job
2. Identify orientation/research/preparation jobs that precede the purchase decision
3. Build content, tools, or services for those upstream jobs
4. Capture attention and trust before the client reaches the purchase decision stage
5. Route them toward your core product naturally

**Examples**:
- Movehub: "10 tips for cheap international moving" article → quiz that becomes a quote request → movers compete for the lead
- Aviasales: before "buy a ticket" is "find the cheapest option" — a search engine with a different business model than resellers
- DomClick (Sberbank): before "get a mortgage" is "choose a neighborhood, estimate costs, select a property" — client spends months on DomClick, constantly funneled toward mortgage application
- Sravni.ru: before choosing insurance/credit, compare all options
- Wedding photographer who built a rating site and placed himself #1
- Kitchen showroom that offers free design project before you buy cabinets
- Career Space: before "find a job" is "plan career, prepare resume, write cover letter"

**Strategic power**: This is the most accessible mechanic for escaping direct competition. It simultaneously creates value, builds an acquisition channel, and establishes trust before the purchase decision.

### Strategy Mechanic 6: Go to the Higher-Level Job (Accelerated Job / Market Disruption)
**What**: Instead of competing for a sub-job, take ownership of the entire big job. All sub-jobs below your level get killed or radically simplified.
**When to use**: When the current job is actually a small part of what the client really cares about, and you can deliver the whole thing.

**Procedure**:
1. Identify what big job your current core job is a sub-job of
2. Assess: can you take ownership of the big job?
3. If yes: kill or radically simplify all sub-jobs below the big job level
4. Change the value proposition from "we help with [sub-job]" to "we guarantee [big-job result]"
5. Often requires business model change (from selling product to selling outcome)

**Examples**:
- Rolls-Royce: from "sell aircraft engines" to "guarantee flight hours" — airlines no longer manage maintenance, parts ordering, or inspections
- Uber/Yandex Taxi: from "provide a car" to "get you from A to B" — kills jobs: learn to drive, buy a car, find parking, maintain the car, deal with road rage
- ChatGPT: from "search, read articles, synthesize" to "get an answer" — kills the entire research sub-graph
- MidJourney: from "learn to draw, sketch, iterate, use Photoshop" to "describe what you want"
- Meal delivery services (e.g., Повар): from "plan meals, shop, cook, clean" to "just eat delicious food"
- Gosuslugi (government services portal): from "visit 10 different agencies" to "one portal for everything"
- MFC (multi-service centers): same principle in physical space
- Yandex Arenda: for landlords, from "find tenants, show apartment, manage contracts" to "just receive rent"

**This is the Christensen disruptive innovation mechanic**, but made operationally precise through the job graph lens. It is the most powerful mechanic for creating entirely new categories and market positions.

### Strategy Mechanic 7: Feature as Form of Value Mechanic
**What**: Remember that features are the delivery vehicle, not the value itself.
A feature is a specific instantiation of a value mechanic.
**When to use**: Always — when choosing what to build.

**Framework**:
- Big Job → Core Job → Value (how much more efficiently) → Feature (through what)
- Features are communicated last, not first
- If you cannot trace a feature back to a job and a value mechanic, question its existence

### Strategy Mechanic 8: Dark Patterns (Know But Use With Caution)
**What**: Manipulation of user behavior to extract business value at the expense of user value.
**Examples**: Swapping button colors, hiding cancellation, inverted opt-in/opt-out, confusing pricing.
**Author's position**: "I don't support dark patterns. I believe in karma. If I deploy a dark pattern to a million users, I create enormous karmic problems. But you need to know they exist."

## Local vs. Global Optimum

### The Trap of Local Optimization
When you focus only on fixing current problems and shipping features for existing segments, you may be climbing the wrong hill. You reach a local maximum — the best possible outcome within your current approach — but a global maximum exists elsewhere that requires fundamentally different choices.

### How to Recognize Local Optimum:
- Revenue growth slows despite continuous improvements
- You're winning every feature comparison but losing market share
- Your segment is shrinking (like the hosting panel company with 50% market share but declining revenue for years — because cloud services and Wix killed the job entirely)
- Competitors with "inferior" products are growing faster (they found a different hill)

### The Hosting Panel Example (Key Case)
A company came for consultation: "We have 50% market share in Germany. Market share is growing. But revenue has been declining for years."

Diagnosis: The market itself is shrinking faster than their share is growing. Why? Products like Wix, Squarespace, Shopify killed the "configure a server" job for non-professional users entirely. For professional users, AWS/Google Cloud/Azure absorbed "configure a server" as a tiny sub-job of a much larger product.

**Lesson**: You can be the undisputed leader of a dying market. Market share is meaningless if the market is evaporating. The strategic question is not "how to get more share" but "is this the right job to compete for?"

### Procedure: Escaping Local Optimum
1. Zoom out: is your market growing or shrinking? Why?
2. Identify who/what is killing the jobs your product serves
3. Assess: can you move to a higher-level job? An adjacent job? A different segment?
4. Be willing to cannibalize your own product before someone else does
5. The Toyota principle: be dissatisfied even in success

## Competitive Differentiation Strategies

### Strategy A: Compete on the Same Job, Better Execution
- Fix more problems, faster delivery, higher quality, lower cost
- Works when: competitors are lazy, market is young, plenty of room for improvement
- Risk: feature parity eventually equalizes, price war begins

### Strategy B: Compete on Different Job Level
- Move upstream (previous job) or downstream (next job)
- Move to higher-level job (accelerated job)
- Works when: the job graph has clear upstream/downstream opportunities
- Advantage: escapes direct competition entirely

### Strategy C: Compete on Different Segment
- Same product, different user population with different expectations
- Works when: you discover a segment where your current product is already "wow" relative to their expectations
- Example: stop chasing Yandex-level clients, focus on first-timers for whom your basic service is amazing

### Strategy D: Compete on Business Model
- Same job, different monetization/delivery model
- Example: Aviasales vs. ticket resellers — search engine model can serve the "find cheapest" upstream job that resellers cannot
- Example: Rolls-Royce "power by the hour" vs. engine sales

## Growth Mechanics

### Growth Through Job Graph Expansion
Every time you extend your product to cover more jobs in the critical sequence (upstream, downstream, or higher-level), you:
1. Increase value (more jobs covered = more efficient for the user)
2. Create acquisition channels (upstream jobs capture attention before purchase decision)
3. Increase retention (switching cost grows with each integrated job)
4. Increase wallet share (can charge for more value delivered)

### Growth Through Segment Expansion
Apply the same value mechanics to adjacent segments with similar jobs but different expectations. Use the mechanics that create the largest positive prediction error for each new segment.

### Growth Through Value Escalation
Continuously raise the bar: fix problems → kill jobs → cover more jobs → move to higher-level jobs. Each step is more value, more differentiation, harder to copy.

## When to Pivot vs. When to Persist

### Pivot Signals:
- Market is shrinking (your job is being killed by someone else)
- You're the leader but revenue still declines (hosting panel case)
- Multiple research cycles show no viable segment for your core job
- Your "strategy" has been "fix bugs and ship features" for 2+ years with no growth inflection

### Persist Signals:
- You found a segment where your product creates real positive prediction error
- Qualitative research shows strong emotional response to your value proposition
- Your mechanics create value that competitors cannot easily copy (unique upstream position, exclusive content, higher-level job coverage)
- Revenue growth matches or exceeds the investment thesis

### Decision Framework:
1. Is the market (total addressable job frequency x willingness to pay) growing or shrinking?
2. Do we have or can we build a defensible advantage for THIS job?
3. Is there a segment where our value delta is large and growing?
4. If all three answers are "no" → pivot. If any answer is "yes" → investigate further before abandoning.

## Strategic Decision Framework: The Complete Algorithm

### Step 1: Diagnose the Problem (Rumelt Principle)
Do NOT skip to solutions. Do NOT just set goals. Start with: "Why is our trend what it is? What are we doing or not doing that produces this result?"

**From "Good Strategy Bad Strategy" (Richard Rumelt)**: Most executives skip diagnosis and jump to ideas and goals. This is the #1 strategic error. Begin with analysis.

### Step 2: Gather Data (OODA — Observe, Orient)
- Qualitative research: segments, jobs, expectations, consideration sets
- Desk research: market trends, regulation, technology shifts
- Expert interviews: industry knowledge, competitor signals
- Competitive analysis: who competes for what jobs, with what mechanics

### Step 3: Map the Job Landscape
- What jobs exist at which levels?
- Who currently serves which jobs?
- Where are the gaps, inefficiencies, or dying jobs?
- Where are expectations rising fastest?

### Step 4: Identify Strategic Options (Apply Mechanics)
For each option, answer:
- Which job will we compete for?
- For which segment?
- With which value mechanic(s)?
- Why will we win? (resources, competencies, business model, positioning)

### Step 5: Choose and Commit
- Strategy is what you DO, not what you plan
- Strategy is also what you DON'T DO — what you say "no" to
- Resources must actually flow to the strategic choice
- If everyone is still fixing bugs for low-margin clients, that IS the strategy regardless of the slide deck

### Step 6: Execute and Monitor
- Track whether the value delta is materializing
- Monitor hedonic adaptation — when does your "wow" become "expected"?
- Watch for job-killing moves by adjacent industries
- Iterate: OODA cycle continues

## Anti-patterns in Product Strategy

### Anti-pattern: Strategy as Beautiful Goals
**What**: "Our strategy: become the #1 electric vehicle company by 2035" (Mercedes-Benz example)
**Why wrong**: Goals say WHERE you want to go. Strategy says HOW you get there given competition, resources, and constraints. Without "how," it's a wish.
**Instead**: For every goal, demand: "What specific actions, in what sequence, using what mechanics, will produce this outcome? Why will this work and not something else?"

### Anti-pattern: Strategy as Art / Secret Knowledge
**What**: "Product strategy is an art that requires intuition and genius."
**Why wrong**: Translates to "I don't know how to do strategy, but I'll make a serious face." Strategy is algorithmic, learnable, and repeatable.
**Instead**: Follow the diagnostic → data → mechanics → decision algorithm. It's hard but it's knowable.

### Anti-pattern: Strategy by Offsite
**What**: Executives go on a retreat, brainstorm for a day, produce a strategy on sticky notes.
**Why wrong**: Usually just dumps the backlog of things people always wanted to do. No diagnosis, no data, no job analysis.
**Instead**: Invest in research before the offsite. Use the offsite for Orient and Decide phases, not Observe.

### Anti-pattern: Ignoring the Diagnosis
**What**: Jumping directly from "something is wrong" to "here's my idea" without understanding WHY things are wrong.
**Why wrong**: Rumelt's core argument — the most expensive strategic error. You solve the wrong problem.
**Instead**: Spend disproportionate time on diagnosis. "Why is our growth stalling?" before "What should we build?"

### Anti-pattern: Confusing Declared Strategy with Actual Strategy
**What**: The slide deck says "Enter Brazil" but all resources go to fixing problems for low-margin Russian clients.
**Why wrong**: Strategy is what you DO. If everyone is doing X, your strategy is X regardless of what the deck says.
**Instead**: Audit resource allocation. Where are people actually spending time? That reveals the real strategy.

### Anti-pattern: Feature-Bug Loop as Strategy
**What**: "We ship features and fix bugs" as the entire product development approach.
**Why wrong**: This is tactical, not strategic. Competitors do the same. No differentiation, no job graph expansion, no market positioning.
**Instead**: Every quarter, ask: "Are we applying mechanics at higher job levels? Are we expanding the job graph? Are we creating value that competitors cannot copy?"

### Anti-pattern: Winning a Dying Market
**What**: Growing market share in a market that is shrinking overall.
**Why wrong**: The hosting panel company had 50% share but declining revenue because the entire job was being killed by cloud services and no-code tools.
**Instead**: Monitor absolute market size, not just share. If the job you serve is being killed, no amount of market share improvement saves you.

## Key Examples from the Course

### Yandex Browser vs. Chrome
- **Context**: Yandex Browser had 36-37% share, Chrome had 42% in Russia
- **Action**: 1.5-year research program, 80+ interviews, multiple quantitative studies, billions in budget
- **Approach**: Systematic research into segments, jobs, value mechanics, barriers to switching
- **Result**: Yandex Browser overtook Chrome by autumn of the prior year
- **Lesson**: Strategic research at scale, methodically applied, beats intuition

### Hosting Panel Company
- **Context**: 50% market share in Germany, revenue declining for years
- **Diagnosis**: Cloud services (AWS, Google Cloud) and no-code tools (Wix, Squarespace) killed the "configure a server" job. For non-professionals, the job doesn't exist. For professionals, it's a nano-job inside cloud platforms.
- **Lesson**: Being a market leader means nothing if the market is disappearing. Strategic question: "Is this the right job to compete for?"

### Yandex Drive (Carsharing)
- **Context**: Deliomobl existed but had terrible product quality
- **Action**: Anton Ryazanov, a regular team lead at Yandex, saw the opportunity. Presented to investment committee: "We have maps, routing, money, product competence, and brand. Let's build carsharing."
- **Result**: One of the best carsharing products in Russia and globally
- **Lesson**: Strategy can be bottom-up. Line employees can identify strategic opportunities that executives miss.

### Rolls-Royce "Power by the Hour"
- **Context**: Selling aircraft engines, airlines manage their own maintenance
- **Action**: Changed business model to sell guaranteed flight hours. Took over all maintenance, parts, inspections.
- **Result**: Airlines' "maintain engines" job was killed entirely. Rolls-Royce escaped commodity competition.
- **Lesson**: Moving to a higher-level job + changing business model = new market category

## Terminology

| Term | Definition | Context |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Value mechanic** | A specific way to increase value by transforming the job graph | Applies to both value creation and strategy |
| **Job graph** | The hierarchical structure of all jobs, sub-jobs, and micro-jobs a person performs to achieve a big job | Foundation for identifying where to apply mechanics |
| **Critical sequence** | The mandatory sequence of jobs that must be performed to complete a big job | Determines upstream/downstream opportunities |
| **Prediction error** | The difference between the brain's automatic prediction and actual reality | Negative = problem, positive = delight |
| **Hedonic adaptation** | The retraining of the brain's prediction model so that yesterday's delight becomes today's expectation | Drives continuous need for value escalation |
| **Accelerated job** | Moving to a higher-level job, killing all sub-jobs below | The most powerful disruption mechanic |
| **Consideration set** | The set of solutions a client considers for a given job | Determines competitive landscape |
| **Dark pattern** | Design manipulation that extracts business value at the expense of user value | Know but use cautiously |
| **OODA cycle** | Observe-Orient-Decide-Act decision loop from US military doctrine | Framework for strategic decision-making |
| **Local optimum** | The best result achievable within current approach, but not the best possible result overall | The trap of optimizing the wrong thing |
| **Expected result criteria** | How the client wants the job done — not just "what" but "how" | Differentiates segments and determines value |
| **Feature parity** | Matching competitors' feature set | Necessary baseline, insufficient for strategy |
| **Vendor lock** | Dependency on a specific vendor that makes switching prohibitively expensive | Both a retention mechanic and a strategic risk |
