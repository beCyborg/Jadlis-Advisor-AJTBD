# AJTBD Terminology (Zamesin v13)

## Metadata
- **Source**: Course "Kak delat produkt" (How to build product), Terminology module
- **Author**: Ivan Zamesin
- **Version**: v13
- **Type**: course glossary
- **Scope**: Complete terminology of the Advanced Jobs To Be Done methodology

## Core Concepts

### JTBD (Jobs To Be Done)
**Definition**: A framework that helps understand the process of a person's transition from "State A" to expected "State B", the motivation for this transition, and how the person makes it happen.
**Context**: The foundational framework upon which AJTBD builds. Literally "jobs that need to be done." People are segmented (grouped) by jobs in JTBD.
**Related**: Advanced JTBD, Job, Job Graph

### Advanced JTBD (AJTBD)
**Definition**: Ivan Zamesin's interpretation and extension layer on top of the standard JTBD framework. Everything in this methodology operates within AJTBD.
**Context**: AJTBD is counter-intuitive and requires "brain rewiring." Theoretical understanding is insufficient -- it requires conducting interviews with a specific script, interpreting results, making decisions, and observing how different solutions work across products.
**Related**: JTBD, Job Graph, Consideration Set

### Job (Work / Job Story / AJTBD Scenario)
**Definition**: A goal or task a person wants to accomplish. The primary unit of analysis in AJTBD.
**Format**:
- **WHEN** I am in [context] + psychological traits + past experience + activating knowledge + trigger
- **I WANT** to achieve [expected result]
- **SO THAT** I get [expected result of the higher-level job] and feel differently in the context of executing the higher-level job
**Context**: Jobs are internal to the person -- they exist in their inner world. Study them through actions: what the person did in the past, what they spent time, money, energy on. Never trust future intentions.
**Related**: Job Graph, Higher-level Job, Lower-level Jobs

### "Hire a Solution"
**Definition**: Choose which actions, product, tool, or service a person will use to execute their job (transition from State A to expected State B).
**Context**: A person "hires" a solution the way you'd hire an employee -- to get a specific job done. The solution can be a product, a service, another person, or the person's own actions ("I'll do it myself").
**Related**: Consideration Set, Job/Solution Cost, Job/Solution Benefits

### Higher-level Job (Job Above)
**Definition**: A job at a higher level in the hierarchy, for which lower-level jobs are executed. The "so that" part of any job description points to its higher-level job.
**Context**: Example: For the higher-level job "change career to frontend developer", a person executes lower-level jobs: "understand what IT professions exist", "figure out which profession suits me", "understand if I can handle it", "get knowledge and skills", "find a job".
**Related**: Lower-level Jobs, Job Graph, Core Job

### Lower-level Jobs (Jobs Below)
**Definition**: The set of jobs a person executes so that the higher-level job gets accomplished.
**Context**: Lower-level jobs can be sequential or frequency-based. The result of the higher-level job is typically obtained at or near the end of the lower-level sequence.
**Related**: Higher-level Job, Sequential Jobs, Frequency Jobs

### Job Graph
**Definition**: A structure/hierarchy of jobs: higher-level jobs, lower-level jobs, jobs the person did in the past, jobs done after a specific job. The second critical unit of analysis in AJTBD (alongside individual jobs).
**Context**: Called a "graph" (not "tree") because it contains many-to-many relationships. One lower-level job can serve multiple higher-level jobs. A single person's graph can contain hundreds of jobs. For a market segment, potentially thousands. You never need to know the entire graph -- only the pieces relevant to your business task.
**Related**: Job, Sequential Jobs, Frequency Jobs, Core Job

### Sequential Jobs
**Definition**: Jobs characterized by step-by-step progression toward the higher-level job result, similar to project execution. The result of one job unlocks the ability to execute the next.
**Context**: Example: project work "build a house" -- person executes sequential jobs: "figure out how much money is needed", "save the required sum", "choose where to buy land", etc. Value/benefit is typically received at or near the END of the sequence. This is critical for product design.
**Related**: Frequency Jobs, Critical Sequence, Job Graph

### Frequency Job
**Definition**: A job executed by a person with some regularity/periodicity (daily, weekly, monthly, yearly).
**Context**: Examples: "drink coffee", "calculate A/B test metrics". Frequency jobs are essential for subscription/recurring business models -- you need segments whose jobs are executed MORE FREQUENTLY than other segments, all else being equal.
**Related**: Sequential Jobs, Job Graph

### Viral Job
**Definition**: A job done FOR another person or TOGETHER with another person. Virality criterion: the second person may start using the product if the first person executes this job with our product.
**Context**: Example: "order a taxi for mom" -- because I ordered a taxi for mom, she learned to use the service and gradually started ordering taxis herself. Products can be built entirely around viral jobs (Figma, Miro, Google Docs). When entering a market, prioritize segments with MORE viral jobs that are executed MORE FREQUENTLY.
**Related**: Job Graph, Frequency Jobs

### Tax Jobs
**Definition**: Jobs that arise because some solution failed to execute (or poorly executed) a job, forcing the person to do additional work to still accomplish the higher-level job.
**Context**: Example: during vacation, passport was stolen -- the tax job "restore stolen passport" appears. People experience irritation and anger from tax jobs because they predicted the solution would work, were wrong, and must now spend additional effort. Tax jobs are a strong signal of product problems and opportunities.
**Related**: Job, Higher-level Job, Barriers to Solution

### False Jobs
**Definition**: Jobs a person SAYS they plan to do in the future. People are very bad at predicting the future and frequently lie to themselves and others. With high probability, these jobs will NOT be executed.
**Context**: Never ask people what they plan to do in the future. Criterion: person speaks about the future. Counter-action: ask "Have you done anything in the past to [achieve this]?" If no past action, treat as false job. Always segment by past behavior (spent time, money, energy).
**Related**: Job, Segmentation

### Sleeping Jobs
**Definition**: Jobs a person has never executed before, but COULD start executing if they receive activating knowledge about a more valuable solution for their higher-level job.
**Context**: Sleeping jobs are directly linked to the existence of a more valuable solution for the higher-level job, but this solution may be unfamiliar. When a person learns about a dramatically better way to accomplish their higher-level job (10x improvement), sleeping jobs "wake up" and the entire sub-graph of lower-level jobs appears.
**Related**: Activating Knowledge, Higher-level Job, Consideration Set

### Activating Knowledge
**Definition**: Knowledge that leads to: awakening a sleeping job, modifying an existing job, or replacing an existing job with a different one. Specifically -- knowledge about the existence of a more effective way to execute the higher-level job via a new type of solution the person didn't know about.
**Context**: Example: for higher-level job "buy a car", person used to do "find a car by myself." Upon learning that car-selection specialists exist (making the process much simpler), the job changes to "find a specialist to help buy a car." The activating knowledge: "the buying process is complex; car-selection specialists exist." Activating knowledge is knowledge about VALUE for the Big Job.
**Related**: Sleeping Jobs, Consideration Set, Orientation Jobs

### Orientation Jobs
**Definition**: Jobs whose purpose is to UPDATE the consideration set and update knowledge about job graphs of different solutions in the consideration set. Identified by verbs: understand, learn, find, choose, figure out.
**Context**: Example: person moving to a new city has orientation job "understand where I will live." They learn options (rent apartment, stay with friends, hotel), evaluate costs/benefits/graphs for each, then choose. The chosen solution "jumps" into the higher-level job and determines the entire subsequent graph of jobs. Orientation jobs are often among the FIRST low-level sequential jobs.
**Related**: Consideration Set, Activating Knowledge, Sequential Jobs

### Consideration Set
**Definition**: A person's knowledge about ALL solutions they could hire for a specific job, plus the perceived value of each solution (costs, benefits, graph complexity, trust, brand loyalty).
**Context**: Factors influencing solution selection from consideration set: habit, past experience, predicted/actual costs (money, time, energy, cognitive load), benefits (resources, emotions, results received), value (delta of benefits minus costs), trust, brand loyalty, reviews/ratings. Often evaluated unconsciously.
**Related**: Orientation Jobs, Activating Knowledge, Job/Solution Cost

### Value of Product for Customer
**Definition**: Executing the customer's jobs in a MORE EFFICIENT way (less costs/expenses, more benefits). Value is created EXCLUSIVELY from knowledge of the job graph and customer needs.
**Context**: Example: solution "take a taxi in the rain" is more valuable than "walk in the rain to the metro" because you won't get wet, won't feel negative emotions, won't get sick, won't jostle with people. Value = reduced total investment + increased total benefits for the higher-level job.
**Related**: Job/Solution Cost, Job/Solution Benefits, Job Graph

### Job/Solution Cost
**Definition**: Total expenditure of time, money, and energy across ALL lower-level jobs when using a specific solution.
**Context**: Example: for job "listen to podcasts during exercise" with solution "Apple Podcasts + Bluetooth headphones", lower-level jobs include: "find headphones", "charge headphones if dead", "find a podcast", "listen to podcast" -- each consuming effort and energy.
**Related**: Job/Solution Benefits, Value, Consideration Set

### Job/Solution Benefits
**Definition**: All resources a person receives from executing a job with a specific solution.
**Context**: Example: when you find and listen to a podcast, you receive ideas, inspiration, answers to questions -- the value from executing the higher-level job.
**Related**: Job/Solution Cost, Value, Consideration Set

### Need (Psychological Need)
**Definition**: Deep, mostly unconscious needs of the brain and psyche described by psychological models (Self-Determination Theory, Maslow's hierarchy). At different times, different need sets are active, but basic needs (contact, safety, respect, growth, autonomy) are active almost always.
**Context**: The brain formulates goals (which ARE jobs in AJTBD) to satisfy needs. The higher up the job graph (closer to needs), the stronger the emotions as feedback signal. Needs drive all jobs but are rarely articulated by the person.
**Related**: Job, Higher-level Job, Emotions

### Feature
**Definition**: A specific part of the product that the client considers valuable. This is a SIGNAL that the client noticed something specific is valuable to them -- NOT the mechanism by which value is created.
**Context**: Features appear in lower-level job descriptions. The lower in the graph, the more product-specific details appear in job descriptions. The higher in the graph, the fewer product details. Do not confuse feature recognition with understanding how value is actually created.
**Related**: Value, Job Graph, Lower-level Jobs

### Aha-moment
**Definition**: Description of the moment AND context when the customer received and consciously recognized the product's value.
**Context**: Used to understand what triggers value recognition. Helps design onboarding and first-use experiences that get users to this moment faster.
**Related**: Value, Critical Sequence

### Opportunity Cost
**Definition**: The foregone benefit that could have been obtained from an alternative decision.
**Context**: Example: on May holidays you chose to binge-watch TV series. The opportunity cost is the knowledge/skills you didn't gain from reading a book, taking a course, or building a pet project. Always consider when evaluating solution choices.
**Related**: Consideration Set, Job/Solution Cost

### Riskiest Assumption Test (RAT)
**Definition**: Meta-framework asserting that everything new we do is based on positively-sounding assumptions, but each has a specific risk of not being confirmed.
**Context**: Example: when creating a new product for an important frequency job, we assume a sufficient number of customers with this important frequency job exists AND we can create significantly more value for them. This riskiest assumption is what we test through qualitative research when seeking a segment. Every business decision chain has a "riskiest" link.
**Related**: Pivot, Top-down approach, Segment

### Pivot
**Definition**: A change in the set of risky assumptions about: the segment with an important frequency job, whether the found segment will buy our solution with our value hypothesis, or whether pessimistic unit economics will work.
**Context**: When any risky assumption fails to confirm, changing the segment, business model, or value hypothesis = changing risky assumptions = pivot. Not about changing the product randomly, but about systematically updating falsified assumptions.
**Related**: Riskiest Assumption Test, Segment, Value Hypothesis

### Bottom-up Approach to PMF
**Definition**: Approach that starts NOT with qualitative research or segment search by jobs, but with building an MVP, trying to sell it, and attracting clients.
**Context**: Start with a working product, then try to sell. Riskier but faster initial signal. Contrast with top-down approach.
**Related**: Top-down Approach, PMF, MVP

### Top-down Approach to PMF
**Definition**: Approach starting with market study and qualitative research: find and select segments, study the job graph, generate a value hypothesis, sell a non-existent product on solution interviews. Only after finding a segment and confirming via sales -- invest in development.
**Context**: Sequence: study market -> qualitative research -> find segments -> study job graph -> generate value hypothesis -> solution interviews (sell non-existent product) -> confirm segment buys -> invest in development.
**Related**: Bottom-up Approach, PMF, Solution Interview, Segment

### Segment
**Definition**: A description of a group of people united by some attribute. The most useful segmentation in AJTBD is segmentation BY JOBS or by a GROUP OF JOBS (which may be part of a job graph).
**Context**: Example: if you recruit members to a business club for these jobs: "hire employees through networking", "drink with people who understand you", "get inspired by cool entrepreneurs" -- they qualify into one segment. Critical rule: segment by PAST BEHAVIOR (the person spent time/money/energy), not by stated future intentions.
**Related**: Core Jobs, Job Graph, ABCD Segmentation

### Target Jobs
**Definition**: Jobs for which our product competes.
**Context**: Identify which jobs in the job graph your product is "hired" to execute. These define your competitive landscape -- you compete not with similar products, but with whatever solution currently executes these jobs (could be Excel, a notebook, a WhatsApp chat, or "doing it myself").
**Related**: Consideration Set, Higher-level Job, Segment

### ABCD Segmentation
**Definition**: Customer segmentation by margin for our business model AND customer satisfaction with our current solution:
- **A**: High-margin. See high value; buy quickly; almost no support tickets.
- **B**: Lower margin than A but still good; product valuable but less than for A; buy slightly slower; generate more support load.
- **C**: Low-margin. Product doesn't satisfy them; purchase likely a mistake; generate many support tickets; long sales cycle.
- **D**: Mistaken buyers. Generate losses and drain sales/support teams.
**Context**: Use to identify which customers to focus on (A/B) and which to actively filter out (C/D). Directly informs product and marketing decisions.
**Related**: Segment, Value, PMF

### Sean Ellis Segmentation
**Definition**: Segment existing customers by answer to: "How disappointed would you be if this product ceased to exist?" Claim: if >40% would be "very disappointed", the product has PMF.
**Context**: Quick PMF signal. Does not replace job-graph-based segmentation but serves as a fast health check.
**Related**: PMF, Segment, ABCD Segmentation

### Barriers to Solution
**Definition**: Fears, anxieties, and doubts that a NEW solution will not optimally execute the job.
**Context**: Barriers prevent switching even when the new solution is objectively better. Address in marketing, onboarding, and activating knowledge delivery. Distinct from barriers to the job itself.
**Related**: Barriers to Job, Drivers, Consideration Set

### Barriers to Job
**Definition**: Fears, anxieties, and doubts that the person won't be able to execute the job effectively enough.
**Context**: Example: "I'm scared to go to a psychotherapist" -- barrier to the job itself, not to any specific solution. Often blocks the entire critical sequence before the person even considers solutions.
**Related**: Barriers to Solution, Critical Sequence, Tax Jobs

### Drivers
**Definition**: Everything that motivates the client to buy or continue using a specific solution.
**Context**: Include in research interviews. Different from value -- drivers are the MOTIVATIONAL forces (emotional and rational) that push toward action.
**Related**: Barriers to Solution, Value, Consideration Set

### Local Optimum
**Definition**: Low-risk product development strategy -- optimizing the existing product without fundamental changes to segment, product, or business model.
**Context**: Example: running an A/B test on a product card page with different loyalty mechanics. Safe, incremental improvement. Limited upside potential.
**Related**: Global Optimum, Strategy

### Global Optimum
**Definition**: High-risk product development strategy where you decide to fundamentally change the segment, high-level job, target country, product concept, or business model.
**Context**: Examples: entering a different country, switching from transactional to subscription business model. High risk, high potential upside.
**Related**: Local Optimum, Pivot, Strategy

### UX Test
**Definition**: A simple tool accessible to any specialist for finding errors in products (landing pages, interfaces, apps, emails, PDFs) in the context of whether a person can execute jobs with our solution.
**Context**: Test whether the person can actually accomplish their jobs through your interface without friction. Not about aesthetics -- about job executability.
**Related**: Value, Job Graph, Critical Sequence

### Expert Interview
**Definition**: Interview with domain experts to obtain knowledge about a market, customers, and products. Often the most relevant experts are founders or employees of competing companies selling similar products in the same market.
**Context**: Part of the research phase. Experts provide market-level knowledge that individual customer interviews cannot.
**Related**: Solution Interview, Problem Interview, Deep Interview

### Solution Interview
**Definition**: Interview to test the risky assumption that a customer from the chosen segment WILL BUY the product with the value hypotheses we've embedded. Goal: understand match or no match -- NOT "sell at any cost."
**Context**: The goal is to understand whether the customer from this segment will buy when we communicate this value proposition at scale. Test the offer, not close a deal.
**Related**: Problem Interview, Riskiest Assumption Test, Value Hypothesis

### Problem Interview
**Definition**: A problem is the consequence of a solution being hired for a job and failing to execute it (or executing poorly). Problem interview = the part of AJTBD interview that studies ONLY jobs where problems exist.
**Context**: Subset of the AJTBD interview focused specifically on dissatisfaction, tax jobs, and failed solutions.
**Related**: Solution Interview, Tax Jobs, Deep Interview

### Deep Interview (Customer Interview)
**Definition**: One-on-one communication with a person, in person or via audio/video call. AJTBD interview IS a deep interview.
**Context**: The primary research method in AJTBD. All other interview types (problem, solution, expert) are variations of this format.
**Related**: Expert Interview, Solution Interview, Problem Interview

### Research Design (Zamesin's)
**Definition**: An algorithm / sequence of actions Zamesin recommends for solving a specific business task. Defines which interview blocks, which questions, and which analysis steps to apply for a given business task.
**Context**: Modular approach -- different business tasks require different modules from the interview guide. Not all questions are needed for every task.
**Related**: Deep Interview, Business Task, Interview Guide

### DEMO Interview
**Definition**: An interview conducted for demonstration purposes of the script/template/practice. Usually a simplified version of a real interview.
**Context**: Training tool for learning the interview methodology.
**Related**: Deep Interview, Interview Guide

### Critical Sequence
**Definition**: The sequence of sequential jobs that a person MUST complete to reach the point where they get value from the higher-level job. If the person drops off at any step, they never reach value.
**Context**: Example: psychotherapy service -- most people drop off at "decide that I need psychotherapy" (step 1-2), long before "find a psychotherapist" (step 5-6). Fix the EARLIEST blocking step in the critical sequence to maximize throughput. The Big Job remains even if person drops off -- they simply go to a competing solution.
**Related**: Sequential Jobs, Value, Barriers to Job

### Core Job (Root Job)
**Definition**: The highest-level job in a relevant portion of the job graph. Does not change for decades or centuries. Contains no product-specific details in its description.
**Context**: Example: "have my own safe home" has not changed for thousands of years. Only the criteria of expected results change over time (warm, electricity, water, enough rooms). Core jobs are close to fundamental needs. The higher the job in the graph, the stronger the emotions, the fewer product details, and the more stable over time.
**Related**: Big Job, Higher-level Job, Needs

### Big Job
**Definition**: The high-level job for which our product and competing solutions actually compete. Higher than our product's direct jobs but lower than the core/root job.
**Context**: Example: mattress company discovered their Big Job is "continue functioning when I'm sleep-deprived at work" -- competing not with other mattresses but with coffee, sleeping pills, quitting the job. Knowing the Big Job reveals the TRUE competitive landscape. Used as anchor for activating knowledge: communicate value for the Big Job, not for lower-level product-specific jobs.
**Related**: Core Job, Target Jobs, Activating Knowledge, Consideration Set
