## Key Processes

### Seven Factors of Conversion

**When**: Diagnosing why visitors do not convert into paying customers.

**Steps**: Walk through all seven factors sequentially. A failure at ANY factor kills the conversion.

1. **Attract the target segment** — visitors must have the job your product performs. Attracting people without the job = zero conversion regardless of everything else.
2. **Offer a solution that is genuinely needed** — the solution must address their core job, not an imaginary problem.
3. **Solution value exceeds competing solutions** — not just "some value" but higher value than the consideration set (competing solutions the lead compares you against).
4. **Lead understood the value** — you may have created valuable solution but explained it poorly: no photos, no video, no clear description of which job is solved and how.
5. **No barriers to the solution AND no barriers to performing the job** — fears, doubts, distrust that (a) your solution will sub-optimally perform the job, or (b) the job cannot be performed at all regardless of which solution is used.
6. **Lead was able to activate into value** — the "aha moment" happened; no 800-field registration forms, no requirement to visit an office, no excessive documentation demands. Minimize friction on the path to experiencing value.
7. **Did not break along the way** — no bugs, layout errors, mobile/desktop issues, broken buttons, confusing navigation. Pure execution quality.

**Notes**: Factors 1-2 belong to product/segment work (earlier workshops). Factors 3-7 are what marketing packaging directly addresses. Do not invest in conversion optimization until you have validated segments and value with actual sales.

### The AJTBD Communication Pyramid

**When**: Building any marketing communication artifact (landing page, banner, sales script, commercial proposal).

**Structure** — communicate in this hierarchy:

1. **Core jobs and micro-jobs we perform** — for the big job. Example: "We will prepare the foundation, build the frame, install the roof — so you build your dream house."
2. **Value for each job** — how we perform each core job better than expectations AND better than competing solutions. Example: "Foundation with waterproofing — zero complaints in 10 years."
3. **Barrier reduction** — if barriers exist, address them explicitly. Example: "Our workers are officially employed, police won't take anyone away. Web cameras on site so you see nobody is slacking."
4. **Features as evidence** — features are NOT the primary message. Features explain WHY we deliver the value. Example: cameras (feature) → you will be calm that workers are working (value) → faster construction (core job outcome).

**Decision Rule**: The causal chain is always Job → Value → Feature, never Feature → Value → Job. Communicate top-down in this hierarchy.

### Banner Text Formula System

**When**: Writing ad banners, social media ads, email subject lines — any short-form marketing copy.

**Steps**: Take elements from the AJTBD job graph and combine them using formulas.

**Formula catalog** (non-exhaustive — combinations are practically infinite):

| Formula | Example (apartment rental product "Khata Bro") |
|---|---|
| Core job + Big job | "Find an apartment where you won't disturb each other working remotely" |
| Core job + Value | "Find an apartment in 30 min from office, learning about best apartments before everyone" |
| Trigger + Core job | "Got a job offer in Moscow? Find an apartment in 30 min from office" |
| Orientation job + First micro-job | "Learn how to rent the right apartment in Moscow without stress. Read the article" |
| Just Big job | "Rent an apartment in Moscow in one week" |
| Just Core job | "Find an apartment in 30 min from your office" |
| Just Micro-job | "Get notifications first about great apartments matching your criteria" |
| Problem with current solution + How we solve the core job | "Found a great apartment but it's already gone? Get curated selections first in Telegram" |
| Trigger + Emotion + Core job | Combine trigger context with emotional state and the core job |
| Problem + Core job | Lead with the pain, follow with the job you solve |
| Micro-job 1 + Micro-job 2 | Chain two sequential micro-jobs from the critical sequence |

**Notes**: You do NOT need creativity. You need the job graph. Take core jobs, micro-jobs, big jobs, value, triggers, barriers, contexts — combine them mechanically. Students who practice this initially feel "this can't be this simple" — it is.

### Landing Page Structure by AJTBD

**When**: Building a product landing page or commercial proposal.

**Structure** (top to bottom):

1. **One-liner** — who you are + core job + value, in one line. Examples:
   - "Kinokhod.ru — online cinema ticket sales" (what: site; core job: buy tickets; value: online)
   - "Skysmart — online tutors for kids" (what: service; core job: hire tutor; value: online)
   - "Miro — collaboration platform" (what: platform; core job: organize teamwork; value: can do it at all)
2. **Big job, Core jobs** — "We will perform THESE core jobs for THESE big jobs with THIS value"
3. **Context / Trigger / Point A** — "We understand you. Recognize yourself." Communicate the trigger situation, problems of the context, emotions. Show the lead you see their world.
4. **Activating knowledge** — fire competitors. Communicate problems with competing solutions. Communicate your advantages. Load the brain with the knowledge that THIS job needs to be performed and that current solutions are inadequate.
5. **Barriers** — "Afraid we'll sub-optimally perform the job? Everything will be fine, don't worry." Address specific fears with specific evidence.
6. **Activation into value** — clear, friction-free path to experiencing the product.

**Notes**: For B2B landing pages, remember there are multiple roles reading the same page (business requester vs. decision maker). Each role has different big jobs and core jobs. The business requester (e.g., marketing manager) cares about "grow conversion, attract new users." The decision maker (e.g., commercial director) cares about "grow revenue." Address both on the landing page.

### Turning Abstract Claims into Concrete Value Communication

**When**: You or your salespeople use words like "fast," "safe," "reliable," "quality," "convenient," "intuitive."

**Steps**:

1. Identify the abstract bullshit word (fast, safe, reliable, quality, convenient, intuitive)
2. Ask the customer (or ask yourself from the customer's perspective): "What does FAST mean to you specifically? HOW fast?"
3. Record the concrete answer:
   - "Fast" → "Takes 10 minutes"
   - "Safe" → "My data is encrypted"
   - "Quality furniture" → "After 10 years the wardrobe looks like new"
   - "Convenient" → "I didn't have to leave the house"
   - "Intuitive" → "My grandmother figured it out in 5 minutes"
4. Use the CONCRETE formulation in all marketing materials, not the abstract word.

**Notes**: This technique works both in research (ask respondents "how exactly?") and when reviewing your own copy. Every abstract adjective must be grounded in a specific, measurable, or experiential criterion. The concrete formulation IS the value communication — either expected value ("I want the wardrobe to look new after 10 years") or experienced value ("Delivered in 10 minutes," "Grandma figured it out in 5 minutes").

### B2B Sales Script Generation (SkySmart Case)

**When**: Selling B2B or high-consideration B2C products where each lead has specific jobs.

**Steps**:

1. Conduct 30-40 JTBD interviews with existing paying customers
2. Identify core jobs, big jobs, barriers, and value for each segment
3. Build a job catalog — a structured list of all discovered jobs with their values
4. Build a sales admin tool where the salesperson selects which specific jobs the current lead has (from the catalog)
5. Generate a personalized pitch that communicates:
   - Value for THIS person's specific core jobs
   - Barrier reduction for THIS person's specific fears
   - Based on the specific child's subject gaps and level (in the SkySmart case)
6. Deliver the personalized pitch

**Result**: SkySmart's average check grew 3.5x. They abandoned the "premium tutor" tier entirely because the standard tier with JTBD-based communication sold dramatically better.

**Notes**: The pitch was generated via a ChatGPT prompt fed with the lead's selected jobs. This was "one big hack" — a prototype, not a polished product. But it worked because the underlying job knowledge was solid.

## Frameworks

### Communication Principle Pyramid

**Purpose**: Ensure every marketing artifact follows the correct hierarchy.

**Structure**:
```
[Core job or Micro-job in focus]
    → How much more valuably we perform it
        → For which Big job
            → To what expected outcome
                → Via which Feature
```

**Decision Criteria**: If you forget features — people don't understand WHAT they're getting (Zamesin's own case: forgot to list features on the Theory tier → sales dropped from 80 to 20-30 seats). If you forget jobs — people don't understand WHY they need it. Every element in the pyramid is necessary.

### Four Forces of Progress Applied to Conversion

**Purpose**: Map conversion factors to the four forces framework.

**Structure**:
- **Push of the situation** (dissatisfaction with current state) → Trigger, Context, Point A
- **Pull of the new solution** (attraction to better outcome) → Value, Core jobs, Big jobs
- **Anxiety of the new solution** (fear of switching) → Barriers to solution
- **Habit of current behavior** (inertia) → Barriers to performing the job; habits cannot be directly influenced through marketing

## Anti-patterns

### Abstract Bullshit Communication
**What**: Using words like "fast," "safe," "reliable," "quality," "convenient," "intuitive" without concrete meaning.
**Why wrong**: These words are too abstract, cause irritation in leads, and fail to differentiate from competitors who use the exact same words.
**Instead**: Ground every abstract claim in a specific outcome criterion from actual customer interviews. "Fast" → "10 minutes." "Safe" → "encrypted data." "Quality" → "looks new after 10 years."

### Feature-First Communication
**What**: Leading with features (cameras, AI, integrations) instead of jobs and value.
**Why wrong**: Customers don't care about features in isolation. Features are evidence, not the message. A camera on a construction site is meaningless — what matters is "your house will be built faster because no one slacks off."
**Instead**: Always lead with the job → value → then mention the feature as proof.

### One Landing Page for All Segments
**What**: Trying to stuff all segments, all jobs, all value propositions into a single landing page.
**Why wrong**: Different segments have different core jobs, different triggers, different barriers. Mixing them dilutes the message for everyone.
**Instead**: One routing landing page + separate funnels with separate landing pages per segment. Main acquisition drives through segment-specific funnels.

### Communicating Regalia Instead of Jobs
**What**: "We are the best," "10 years of experience," "awarded by Forbes," "trusted by thousands" — leading with credentials.
**Why wrong**: A specific person with a specific context, specific land, specific problems does not care about your awards. They care that you solve THEIR specific job.
**Instead**: Lead with their specific job in their specific context. Use social proof and credentials only for barrier reduction, not as the main message.

### Creating Extra Work for the Customer
**What**: Your solution, while valuable, requires the customer to do new tasks they didn't do before (check homework, give feedback to employees, learn a new system).
**Why wrong**: People in general don't want to work more. If your solution adds jobs instead of removing them, the perceived value drops or inverts. Typical B2B mistake.
**Instead**: Communicate "we do everything for you" value. Minimize new obligations. If new work is unavoidable, explicitly address it and show the net gain.

### Premature Conversion Optimization
**What**: Investing in landing page optimization, A/B testing, and conversion hacking before you have validated segments and value.
**Why wrong**: Without knowing your segments and having proven value through actual sales, any optimization is built on sand.
**Instead**: First find segment → create value → validate with sales → THEN optimize conversion using the mechanics from this workshop.

## Examples

### SkySmart Tutor Platform
**Context**: Online tutoring platform had premium tutors that wouldn't sell. Standard pricing, unclear value proposition.
**Action**: Conducted 40 JTBD interviews with paying parents. Identified segments by child's subject, grade, and gap from curriculum. Built a sales admin that generated personalized pitches per lead based on their specific jobs and child's specific gaps. Rewrote the landing page entirely using AJTBD job graph.
**Result**: Average check grew 3.5x. Premium tier was abandoned because standard sales with JTBD communication outperformed it.
**Lesson**: Knowing jobs + communicating through jobs to specific segments = dramatic conversion and revenue lift without changing the product.

### Khata Bro (Fictional Example)
**Context**: Chat bot in Telegram + web aggregator for apartment rental in Moscow. Core jobs: understand how to rent in Moscow + find the best listing quickly with minimal stress. Big job: rent apartment in Moscow within 30 min from office, under 60K rubles, within one week.
**Action**: Applied banner formulas mechanically — core job + big job, trigger + core job, problem + solution, etc. Generated 8+ banner texts in minutes.
**Result**: Demonstrated that writing marketing copy is an algorithm, not creative inspiration. You take the job graph and combine elements by formula.
**Lesson**: Marketing texts are not creativity. They are combinatorial application of job graph elements to formula templates.

### Zamesin's Own Course Pricing Tier
**Context**: Course has two tiers — Standard Plus and Practice. After rewriting the landing page "by jobs," the Practice tier sold well but Standard Plus dropped from 80 to 20-30 seats.
**Action**: Investigated through interviews and CRM analysis. Discovered that while jobs were well-communicated for Practice tier, the Standard Plus tier was missing feature descriptions — people didn't know WHAT they were getting (workshops, recorded sessions, etc.).
**Result**: Added feature descriptions back → sales recovered.
**Lesson**: Every element in the communication pyramid is necessary. Forgetting features is as bad as forgetting jobs. Jobs without features = "sounds nice but what do I actually get?"

## Terminology

| Term | Definition | Context |
|------|-----------|---------|
| One-liner (van-liner) | One-line description: what you are + core job + value | First thing on landing page, concise explanation of the product |
| Activating knowledge | Information that makes the lead understand they need to perform the job AND that current solutions are inadequate | Middle section of landing page; "firing competitors" |
| Communication pyramid | Hierarchy: core job → value → big job → expected outcome → feature | Guides the structure of all marketing artifacts |
| Consideration set | Set of competing solutions the lead will compare you against | Must know this to communicate relative value |
| Orientation job | The job of understanding HOW to perform the core job | Addressed via content (articles, videos, checklists) — lead magnets |
| Lead magnet | Free content that solves the orientation job, pulling leads into the funnel | Example: "Learn how to rent the right apartment in Moscow — download the checklist" |
| Barrier to solution | Fear that YOUR specific solution will sub-optimally perform the job | Addressed with social proof, credentials, guarantees |
| Barrier to job | Fear that the job CANNOT be performed regardless of solution | Addressed with education, reassurance, case studies |
| Point A → Point B | Lead's current state (trigger, context, problems) → desired state (big job outcome) | Landing page should paint the journey from A to B |
